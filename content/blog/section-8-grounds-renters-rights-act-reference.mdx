# Section 8 Grounds Under the Renters' Rights Act: The Complete Reference

Phase 1 of the Renters' Rights Act 2025 came into force on 1 May 2026. Section 21 — the 'no-fault' notice that ended most assured shorthold tenancies for nearly thirty years — is gone for new and existing tenancies in the private rented sector in England. Every possession claim a private landlord now brings runs through Section 8 of the Housing Act 1988, on one or more of the statutory grounds set out in Schedule 2 to that Act, as amended by the Renters' Rights Act.

This is a reference. Bookmark it. If you are working out which ground fits your situation — selling up, moving back in, chasing arrears, dealing with antisocial behaviour — read the relevant section and click through to the legislation and the official GOV.UK guidance before you serve anything.

<Callout variant="info">This article covers possession of assured tenancies by **private landlords in England**. Different rules apply to Private Registered Providers of social housing (the changes start applying to them in 2027), to Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, and to a handful of tenancy types (lodgers, holiday lets, very high-rent tenancies above £100,000 a year) outside Section 8 altogether. The official GOV.UK overview is [here](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/repossessing-your-privately-rented-property-after-1-may-2026).</Callout>

## The headline change

The statutory route to possession is now narrow and reason-based. GOV.UK puts it plainly:

<LegalBlock citation="Repossessing your privately rented property after 1 May 2026, GOV.UK">Since 1 May 2026 you can only give your tenant a notice under section 8 of the Housing Act 1988 to end the tenancy. This is often referred to as a section 8 notice or notice of possession.</LegalBlock>

That means three practical things for a small private landlord. First, you need a **ground** — a statutory reason — and you need to set it out in the notice. Second, you must use the correct **form** (Form 3A, or a form substantially to like effect) and give the **correct notice period** for the ground you are relying on. Third, the court will only make a possession order if you can **prove** the ground, and — for most grounds — only if you have **complied with deposit protection** and any other pre-conditions attached to that particular ground. The detail is in the [grounds for possession guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/grounds-for-possession-guidance-for-landlords-and-letting-agents/grounds-for-possession-guidance-for-landlords-and-letting-agents) and in [Schedule 1 to the Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/26/schedule/1), which rewrites Schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1988.

<PullQuote>No ground, no possession. The notice is only as strong as the reason behind it.</PullQuote>

## Mandatory vs discretionary: the distinction that matters

Every Section 8 ground is either **mandatory** or **discretionary**.

- **Mandatory.** If you prove the ground, the court **must** make a possession order. Judicial discretion is removed once the facts are made out. Most of the grounds a small landlord will actually use — selling, moving in, serious rent arrears — are mandatory.
- **Discretionary.** Even if you prove the ground, the judge will only make an order if it is **reasonable** to do so. The court weighs your evidence against the tenant's circumstances. These grounds are harder to win on and the outcome is less predictable.

The two routes are governed by the same notice and the same form, but they behave very differently in court. Where a discretionary ground is used, the judge can also delay the eviction date beyond the standard 14 days, suspend the possession order on conditions, or refuse it outright. The GOV.UK guidance confirms the distinction directly.

## Pre-conditions that apply across (almost) all grounds

Before you reach for a ground, check the gating requirements. The court will not make a possession order on most grounds unless:

- the deposit has been protected in a [government-approved scheme](https://www.gov.uk/tenancy-deposit-protection),
- you have complied with the scheme's initial requirements, and
- you served the [prescribed information](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2007/797/article/2/made) on the tenant.

If the deposit was not protected correctly, you generally either need to return it (in full, or with agreed deductions) before applying, or wait for any tenant deposit claim to be decided, settled or withdrawn. **Two important exceptions:** the deposit rules do not apply to **Ground 7A** or **Ground 14** (antisocial behaviour). Beyond deposits, a handful of grounds need 'prior notice' — written notice given to the tenant at the start of the tenancy that you may one day use that ground. Examples include the superior-lease grounds (2ZA, 2ZB), the new-students HMO ground (4A), and the employment-end ground (5C).

<Callout variant="warning">Grounds 1 and 1A (moving in or selling) cannot be used in the first **12 months** of a new tenancy. You can serve the notice earlier, but the date by which the tenant must leave has to fall after they have been in the property for 12 months. This is a hard statutory bar.</Callout>

## Mandatory grounds — the reference

Notice periods below come from the [GOV.UK grounds guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/grounds-for-possession-guidance-for-landlords-and-letting-agents/grounds-for-possession-guidance-for-landlords-and-letting-agents). Statutory wording lives in [Schedule 1 to the Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/26/schedule/1), which inserts and amends grounds in Schedule 2 to the Housing Act 1988.

### Ground 1 — occupation by landlord or close family

**When it applies.** You, or a close family member, intend to move into the property as your only or principal home. 'Close family member' is defined in the statute (spouse, civil partner, cohabiting partner, parent, grandparent, sibling, child or grandchild — including step-relations).

**Restrictions.** Cannot be used in the first 12 months of the tenancy. Cannot be combined with re-letting the property — if you use Ground 1 and then put it back on the rental market within the prohibited period, you risk a financial penalty under the Act's enforcement regime.

**Notice period.** 4 months.

**Evidence the court will want.** Something concrete tying the named family member to the move — a signed statement, proof they are leaving previous accommodation, removal arrangements, mortgage or insurance updates.

### Ground 1A — sale of the dwelling-house

**When it applies.** You intend to sell the property (or grant a lease of more than 21 years not at a rack rent). Created specifically by the Renters' Rights Act to replace the 'I want to sell' use of Section 21.

**Restrictions.** Cannot be used in the first 12 months. Cannot be used if your tenant is on a pre-1 May 2026 'lifetime' assured (non-shorthold) tenancy. Re-letting the property within the prohibited post-eviction window risks financial penalty.

**Notice period.** 4 months.

**Evidence.** Estate agent instruction letters, marketing material, an offer letter, an EPC order, a memorandum of sale — anything that shows the sale is real and not a pretext. Judges have been signalled to scrutinise this ground carefully.

### Ground 1B — sale under a Rent to Buy scheme

**When it applies.** The property is part of a Rent to Buy scheme, you have offered the tenant the chance to buy it at the end of the scheme, and they have not bought it. Narrow ground; rarely relevant to small private landlords.

**Notice period.** 4 months.

### Ground 2 — sale by mortgagee

**When it applies.** Your lender (typically a bank, on a default) wants possession to sell the property. The tenant was granted the tenancy at a time when the mortgage was already in place.

**Notice period.** 4 months.

### Grounds 2ZA, 2ZB, 2ZC, 2ZD — superior lease ending

**When they apply.** You sublet under a lease (the 'superior lease') that is itself ending, or that has already ended and reverted to the freeholder. These are specialist grounds — most relevant to agricultural landlords, supported accommodation providers and council-linked companies, or to freeholders who become direct landlords after a long lease (over 21 years) falls in.

**Notice period.** 4 months across all four sub-grounds.

**Prior notice.** Some of these grounds require you to have told the tenant at the start of the tenancy that the ground might be used.

### Ground 4 — student accommodation (institutional)

**When it applies.** Used only by universities and colleges, for property that has been let to students within the 12 months before the tenancy started. Not for ordinary private student lets.

**Notice period.** 2 weeks.

### Ground 4A — student HMOs for new students

**When it applies.** Private landlord with an HMO let to full-time students who needs the property back for a new cohort in line with the academic year. Possession date must fall between **1 June and 30 September**. Tenancy must not have been agreed more than six months before it started.

**Prior notice required:** yes — you must have told the tenants at the outset that you might rely on this ground.

**Notice period.** 4 months.

### Ground 5 — ministers of religion

Property held for use by a minister of religion and needed for that purpose again. **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 5A — occupation by agricultural worker

Property needed to house an employed or self-employed agricultural worker. **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 5B — occupation by person meeting employment requirements

Key-worker-style lets where the existing tenant no longer meets the employment criteria and you want to let to someone who does. Primarily relevant to social and key-worker landlords. **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 5C — end of employment by the landlord

Renumbered from the old Ground 16 and upgraded to **mandatory**. Where the tenancy was tied to the tenant's employment with you and the employment, or the purpose of the accommodation, has ended. **Prior notice required.** **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 5D — end of employment requirements

Paired with 5B/5C for situations where the employment requirements attached to the property no longer apply. **2 months' notice.**

### Grounds 5E and 5F — supported accommodation

For providers of supported accommodation needing to re-let to another person eligible for support, or to end use as supported accommodation. Niche; specialist sector only. **Notice periods vary** — check the GOV.UK guidance.

### Ground 5G — tenancy granted for homelessness duty

Where the tenancy was granted to discharge a local housing authority's homelessness duty and the duty has now come to an end. Almost always a social-landlord ground in practice. **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 5H — 'stepping stone' accommodation

Narrow ground covering accommodation let on a 'stepping stone' basis to people meeting specific eligibility criteria (e.g. recent care leavers). **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 6 — redevelopment

Major works that cannot reasonably be done with the tenant in occupation, where you cannot recover possession by other means (for instance, by negotiating a temporary move). Used very rarely by small private landlords. **4 months' notice.**

### Ground 6A — decant accommodation

New ground covering the use of replacement accommodation provided while redevelopment is carried out. Mostly relevant to registered providers. **4 months' notice.**

### Ground 6B — compliance with enforcement action

New ground letting you recover possession where you need to comply with enforcement action — for example, a banning order, a prohibition order under the Housing Act 2004, or a similar regulatory step that makes continued letting unlawful. **4 months' notice.**

### Ground 7 — death of the tenant

**When it applies.** The sole tenant has died and the tenancy passed by will or intestacy (not by statutory succession to a spouse/partner). Proceedings must be begun within 12 months of the death (or of the landlord becoming aware of it).

**Notice period.** 2 months.

### Ground 7A — severe antisocial or criminal behaviour

**When it applies.** The tenant — or someone living with or visiting them — has been **convicted** of a serious offence, breached an injunction, breached an antisocial-behaviour-related order, or the property has been subject to a closure order preventing access for more than 48 hours.

**Notice period.** Effectively none — you can apply to the court straight away. However, the court cannot make a possession order until **14 days** have passed from the date the notice was served.

**Deposit protection rules do not apply** to this ground. Mandatory, but evidence-heavy: you need the conviction, the order, or the closure order on paper.

### Ground 7B — no right to rent

**When it applies.** The Secretary of State has served notice that the tenant (or one of joint tenants) does not have a right to rent under immigration law. See the [right to rent guidance](https://www.gov.uk/prove-right-to-rent).

**Notice period.** 2 weeks.

### Ground 8 — rent arrears (the mandatory arrears ground)

**When it applies.** The tenant owes **at least 3 months' rent** if rent is paid monthly, or at least **13 weeks' rent** if rent is paid weekly or fortnightly. The threshold was previously two months; the Renters' Rights Act raised it.

**Critical timing.** The arrears must reach the threshold **both at the date of service of the notice and at the date of the hearing**. If the tenant pays the arrears down below the threshold before the hearing, the ground falls away — even if the arrears built back up after the notice was served.

**Universal Credit carve-out.** Arrears that exist because the tenant has not yet received their Universal Credit are **not counted** towards the threshold. This is an important change in practice — verify what the tenant's UC position actually is before serving on Ground 8 alone.

**Notice period.** 4 weeks.

## Discretionary grounds — the reference

### Ground 9 — suitable alternative accommodation

You have arranged suitable alternative accommodation that will be available when the order takes effect. Rare in the private sector. **2 months' notice.**

### Ground 10 — any rent arrears

Any amount of rent unpaid — both at the date the notice was served and at the date proceedings began. Often pleaded alongside Ground 8 as a 'backup' if the arrears might dip below the Ground 8 threshold by the hearing. **4 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 11 — persistent delay in paying rent

The tenant has **persistently** been late paying rent, whether or not any rent is owed at the date of the hearing. The classic 'chronic late payer who always catches up' scenario. **4 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 12 — breach of the tenancy agreement

The tenant has breached an obligation of the tenancy other than the obligation to pay rent — e.g. unauthorised subletting, breach of a no-pet clause that is still lawful under the Act, breach of an occupancy limit. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 13 — deterioration of the property

The condition of the dwelling, or of the common parts, has deteriorated because of acts of waste, neglect or default by the tenant or by a person residing in the property whom the tenant has failed to take steps to control. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 14 — antisocial behaviour

The tenant — or a person residing in or visiting the property — has engaged in conduct that is **capable of causing nuisance or annoyance** to anyone with a right to live in the locality, to the landlord, or to anyone working in the locality; or has been convicted of using the property for illegal or immoral purposes, or of an indictable offence committed in the locality.

**Notice period.** No notice period — you can apply to the court immediately, although the court will not make an order until **14 days** after service. **Deposit protection rules do not apply** to Ground 14.

The Renters' Rights Act added a list of statutory factors the court must consider when deciding whether to make a possession order on an antisocial-behaviour ground — set out in [section 4 of the Act](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/26/section/4). Expect judges to weigh the impact on neighbours and witnesses explicitly.

### Ground 14A — domestic abuse

For use where the tenant has perpetrated domestic abuse against a partner or family member who has been driven from the home and is unlikely to return. Currently available only to social landlords in full; private-landlord use is more limited. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 14ZA — rioting

The tenant or another adult living in the property has been convicted of an indictable offence committed during a riot in the United Kingdom. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 15 — deterioration of furniture

Furniture you provided has deteriorated because of ill-treatment by the tenant or someone they have failed to take steps to control. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 17 — false statement to obtain the tenancy

The tenancy was granted because the tenant — or someone acting at their instigation — knowingly or recklessly made a false statement. The classic 'fake referencing' ground. **2 weeks' notice.**

### Ground 18 — supported accommodation: failure to engage

Tenant in supported accommodation has unreasonably failed to engage with the support being offered. Sector-specific. **4 weeks' notice.**

## Common landlord scenarios — which ground to use

This is where the reference becomes practical. The four scenarios below cover most situations a small private landlord will face.

<StepList>
<Step number={1}>**You want to sell the property.** Ground 1A. Mandatory. 4 months' notice. Cannot be used in the first 12 months of the tenancy. Have your estate-agent paperwork ready — courts will look for evidence the sale is real. Be aware of the post-eviction re-letting restriction.</Step>
<Step number={2}>**You (or a close family member) want to move in.** Ground 1. Mandatory. 4 months' notice. Cannot be used in the first 12 months of the tenancy. Name the family member in the notice and be ready to show their planned move is genuine.</Step>
<Step number={3}>**The tenant is in serious rent arrears.** Plead **Ground 8** (mandatory: 3 months / 13 weeks at notice and hearing) **and Ground 10** (any arrears, discretionary) **and Ground 11** (persistent late payment, discretionary) together. 4 weeks' notice. Check Universal Credit position before relying on Ground 8 alone — UC-related arrears don't count.</Step>
<Step number={4}>**Antisocial behaviour by the tenant or their visitors.** Ground 14 (discretionary) is the workhorse — no waiting period, but you'll need solid evidence: incident logs, neighbour statements, police references, ASB case-management records. Ground 7A (mandatory) is reserved for the most serious cases — typically a conviction or breached order — and is hard to make out without that paper trail.</Step>
<Step number={5}>**The tenant has died and the property hasn't passed to a statutory successor.** Ground 7. Mandatory. 2 months' notice. Begin proceedings within 12 months of the death or your becoming aware of it.</Step>
<Step number={6}>**The tenant has breached the agreement in a non-rent way** (e.g. unauthorised subletting). Ground 12. Discretionary. 2 weeks' notice. Keep documentary evidence of the breach — listings, photos, correspondence.</Step>
</StepList>

## A worked example

Take a landlord in Manchester with a 3-bed terrace let to a single household. The tenancy started in September 2025, so by May 2026 the tenants are eight months in. Rent is monthly. The landlord wants to sell because their own circumstances have changed.

They cannot serve a Ground 1A notice today and have it land cleanly: even with 4 months' notice, the earliest the possession date can fall is January 2027 — but the 12-month restriction means the possession date must also be after September 2026 (the tenancy's first anniversary). January 2027 satisfies both. The landlord serves Form 3A in late May 2026 with a notice date of late September 2026 at the earliest, gathers their estate-agent instruction and marketing pack as evidence, and only applies to court if the tenants haven't left by the notice expiry date.

If in the meantime the tenants fall three months into arrears, the picture changes. The landlord can serve a **second** notice under Grounds 8, 10 and 11 — 4 weeks' notice — and bring that claim faster than the Ground 1A claim. The two notices and two routes to possession can run in parallel; the landlord uses whichever proves out first.

## What can still go wrong

A section 8 claim is technical. The common failure modes are:

- **The deposit isn't properly protected** — and you cannot get past the gateway for any ground except 7A or 14.
- **The notice is on the wrong form, names the wrong ground, or sets out the ground incorrectly** — the court can dismiss the claim or send you back to the start. GOV.UK is explicit that the substance of the ground and the reasons it applies must be set out in full.
- **You served before a 'prior notice' requirement was met** — fatal for several of the niche grounds.
- **You served Ground 1 or 1A inside the 12-month bar** — the date in the notice has to be after the 12-month point.
- **You relied on Ground 8 alone, and the arrears dipped below the threshold before the hearing** — the mandatory ground falls away. This is why experienced landlords plead Grounds 8, 10 and 11 together.

<Callout variant="tip">If your claim depends on antisocial-behaviour evidence, start the evidence file before you serve. Dated incident logs, neighbour witness statements, police incident numbers and any ASB case-management correspondence carry far more weight than a recollection given at the hearing.</Callout>

## How to actually serve and apply

The procedural side is covered in our companion guide on [how to end a tenancy after Section 21](https://landlord-ready.com/how-to-end-tenancy-after-section-21-abolished), but in brief:

1. Complete **Form 3A** (or a form substantially to like effect). The current watermarked version sits on the [assured tenancy forms page](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/assured-tenancy-forms-for-privately-rented-properties-from-1-may-2026).
2. Set out **every ground** you intend to rely on and the full substance of each. Pleading additional grounds in the alternative is generally good practice.
3. Serve the notice in accordance with the tenancy agreement and keep a dated copy plus proof of service.
4. If the tenant does not leave, apply to the county court. Use the [Possession Claim Online (PCOL) service](https://www.gov.uk/possession-claim-online-recover-property) for rent-only claims, or the paper N5 + N119 route for everything else. The court fee is currently £404.
5. Prepare evidence in support of every ground pleaded.

<PullQuote>The grounds are the case. The notice is just the gateway. Build your evidence file before you serve, not after.</PullQuote>

## When to get advice

A Section 8 reference will get you a long way, but it will not answer the question of whether a particular ground is winnable on the facts of your case. If you are facing antisocial behaviour, a contested arrears claim, or a tenant who has a credible counterclaim — for disrepair, for example — talk to a housing solicitor or your landlord association's legal team before you serve. A defective notice usually costs you another four weeks to four months, plus the court fee, plus your time. Getting the ground right first time is the cheap option.
