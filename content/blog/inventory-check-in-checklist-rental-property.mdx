## Why a Good Inventory Is Your Best Insurance

When a tenancy ends and there is a dispute over the deposit, the adjudicator at the deposit protection scheme will ask one simple question: what is the evidence? If you cannot demonstrate the condition of the property at the start of the tenancy, you will struggle to prove that any damage occurred during it.

This is not a theoretical risk. The burden of proof in deposit disputes falls squarely on the landlord under [government tenancy deposit rules](https://www.gov.uk/tenancy-deposit-protection). The three [government-approved deposit protection schemes](https://www.gov.uk/deposit-protection-schemes-and-landlords) consistently report that claims fail most often because the landlord's evidence is incomplete, undated, or missing entirely. A comprehensive inventory carried out on check-in day is the single most effective thing you can do to protect your position.

<PullQuote>In a deposit dispute, the adjudicator does not visit the property. They decide based entirely on the paperwork in front of them. Your inventory is your case.</PullQuote>

## What Your Inventory Should Cover

A proper inventory is more than a list of furniture. It is a detailed record of the condition of every surface, fixture, fitting, and appliance in the property. You are creating a snapshot that can be compared against the same property at check-out, potentially years later.

### Room-by-Room Checklist

Work through every room methodically. For each room, record the condition of:

- **Walls** — note the colour, finish (paint, wallpaper, tiles), and any marks, scuffs, cracks, or holes
- **Ceilings** — note any stains, cracks, peeling paint, or signs of damp
- **Floors** — record the type (carpet, laminate, tile, vinyl) and condition, including stains, scratches, or worn areas
- **Windows** — check the glass, frames, handles, locks, and any condensation or mould around seals
- **Doors** — note the condition of the door itself, the frame, handles, locks, and hinges
- **Light fittings** — record the type and confirm all bulbs are working
- **Sockets and switches** — note the number and condition in each room
- **Curtains and blinds** — record the type, colour, and condition, including any staining or damage to mechanisms
- **Built-in furniture** — wardrobes, shelving, cupboards — open every door and check inside

### Kitchen Specifics

The kitchen deserves particular attention because it contains the highest concentration of items that can be damaged or go missing:

- Worktops — note any chips, burns, stains, or scratches
- Sink and taps — record the condition and check for drips
- Oven, hob, and extractor — clean or not, any damage, brand and model
- Fridge-freezer — condition of shelves, drawers, seals
- Washing machine and dishwasher — brand, model, serial number, condition
- All cupboard interiors — open every one

### Bathroom Specifics

- Bath, shower tray, and basin — chips, stains, sealant condition
- Toilet — seat, flush mechanism, any staining
- Tiles and grouting — cracks, mould, missing grout
- Extractor fan — working or not
- Mirrors and cabinets — condition and fixings

### Garden and External Areas

This is one of the most commonly overlooked areas. If your property has a garden, patio, or any external space, document it with the same care as the interior:

- Lawn condition — healthy, patchy, overgrown
- Borders, hedges, and trees — trimmed or not, general state
- Fencing and gates — condition, any broken panels
- Patio or decking — stains, moss, damage
- Shed or outbuilding — contents, condition, lock
- Bins — number and type provided

<Callout variant="warning">If you do not document the garden at check-in, you will find it very difficult to claim for garden restoration at check-out. Adjudicators regularly reject garden-related claims for lack of baseline evidence.</Callout>

## Photography Best Practices

Written descriptions are essential, but photographs are what win deposit disputes. A well-taken set of check-in photos can be worth more than pages of written notes.

### How to Take Effective Inventory Photos

- **Shoot wide and close.** For each room, take a wide-angle shot from the doorway showing the whole space, then close-up shots of any existing damage, marks, or wear.
- **Use good lighting.** Open curtains, turn on lights. Dim photos obscure detail and weaken your evidence.
- **Ensure timestamps are visible.** Use a camera or phone app that embeds the date and time in the image metadata. Some landlords place a dated newspaper in the first photo of each room as additional proof.
- **Photograph every wall.** Stand in the centre of each room and photograph all four walls systematically.
- **Capture existing damage in detail.** Scratches on worktops, chips on bath enamel, stains on carpet — photograph everything, no matter how minor. If it is not photographed, it does not exist for dispute purposes.
- **Include appliance serial numbers.** Take a close-up of the rating plate on every appliance, showing the brand, model, and serial number.
- **Do not forget ceilings.** Especially in bathrooms and kitchens where staining or damp is common.

<Callout variant="info">Store your photos in a dedicated folder named with the property address and check-in date. Back them up to cloud storage immediately. Photos stored only on a phone that gets lost or replaced are no use to anyone.</Callout>

## Meter Readings

On check-in day, read every utility meter at the property and record the readings in your inventory:

- **Gas meter** — note the reading and meter serial number
- **Electricity meter** — note the reading (day and night if it is an Economy 7 meter) and serial number
- **Water meter** — if the property has one, record the reading

Photograph each meter showing the reading clearly. This protects both you and the tenant — it ensures the tenant is only billed for energy used during their tenancy, and it avoids disputes with utility companies.

## Keys and Security

Document every key and security device handed to the tenant:

- Number of front door keys
- Number of back door keys
- Window keys
- Garage or shed keys
- Fob or code for communal entry systems
- Alarm codes and instructions

Record the number of each type of key in the inventory and have the tenant acknowledge receipt. Lost keys are a legitimate deduction from the deposit, but only if you can prove how many were provided at the start.

## Schedule of Condition vs Full Inventory

You may come across two terms used somewhat interchangeably, but they serve different purposes:

A **full inventory** lists every item in the property along with its condition. This is appropriate for furnished or part-furnished lettings where you are providing furniture, kitchenware, appliances, and other items that the tenant will use.

A **schedule of condition** records the state of the property's fixtures, fittings, and surfaces without listing individual moveable items. This is more suitable for unfurnished lettings where you are providing the bare property.

For most small landlords letting a furnished or part-furnished property, a full inventory is the safer choice. If you are letting unfurnished, a schedule of condition is usually sufficient — but make sure it is detailed enough to cover every surface and fitting.

## Getting the Tenant to Sign

Once the inventory is complete, provide a copy to the tenant and ask them to review and countersign it. This step is important for two reasons.

First, a countersigned inventory carries significantly more weight in a deposit dispute. It demonstrates that the tenant agreed with your assessment of the property's condition at the start of the tenancy.

Second, it gives the tenant a fair opportunity to note anything they disagree with or any damage you may have missed. Most [deposit protection schemes](https://www.gov.uk/deposit-protection-schemes-and-landlords) recommend giving the tenant seven days to review the inventory and raise any amendments.

<PullQuote>A signed inventory is a mutual agreement about the starting condition of the property. Without it, the tenant can simply dispute your version of events.</PullQuote>

If the tenant refuses to sign, note that refusal in writing and send them a copy of the inventory by email so you have a record of delivery. An unsigned inventory is still valid evidence — it simply carries less weight than one the tenant has acknowledged.

## Digital vs Paper Inventories

There is no legal requirement to use a particular format. What matters is that the inventory is detailed, dated, and supported by photographs.

**Paper inventories** work perfectly well. Print two copies, walk through the property with the tenant, both sign, each keep a copy. The disadvantage is that paper can be lost, and linking printed photos to specific rooms can be cumbersome.

**Digital inventories** are increasingly common and offer practical advantages. You can embed photographs directly alongside written descriptions, timestamp everything automatically, and store copies securely in the cloud. Several apps and software tools are designed specifically for property inventories.

**Professional inventory services** are another option worth considering. An independent inventory clerk will carry out the check-in, produce a detailed report with photographs, and provide an impartial record. This typically costs between £80 and £200 depending on the size of the property. The advantage is that the report comes from a neutral third party, which can carry additional weight in disputes. If you are [managing your property without a letting agent](/blog/managing-rental-property-without-letting-agent), this is one task where paying a professional can be a sound investment.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even experienced landlords can undermine their own evidence by overlooking the basics:

- **Not photographing the garden.** As noted above, this is one of the most frequent omissions and one of the hardest to recover from in a dispute.
- **Not recording appliance serial numbers.** If an appliance is swapped or damaged during the tenancy, you need to prove what was originally provided.
- **Not dating photographs.** Photos without metadata or a visible date reference have limited evidential value. An adjudicator needs to be satisfied that the photos were taken at the start of the tenancy, not at some other time.
- **Being too vague.** "Good condition" means nothing. "Magnolia painted walls, small scuff mark (approximately 2cm) on wall adjacent to door frame at waist height" is evidence.
- **Not checking inside cupboards and drawers.** Damage inside kitchen cupboards, bathroom cabinets, and wardrobes is common and easy to miss at check-in.
- **Forgetting smoke and carbon monoxide alarms.** Record that they are present, working, and tested on check-in day. This also supports your compliance with [landlord safety responsibilities](/blog/landlord-responsibilities-repairs-maintenance).
- **Not keeping a copy.** Whether digital or paper, always retain your own copy of the signed inventory and all photographs. Do not rely on the tenant having theirs.

## Bringing It All Together

A thorough check-in inventory takes time — typically one to two hours for a standard two-bedroom property. It can feel like an administrative burden, especially when you are keen to hand over the keys and get the tenancy started. But the hour you spend documenting the property at the beginning could save you hundreds or thousands of pounds at the end.

Approach it systematically. Work room by room, photograph everything, record every detail, and get the tenant's signature. Keep your records safe and accessible. When the tenancy ends and you need to compare the property's condition, you will have everything you need to make a fair and evidenced assessment — and if a dispute arises, you will be in the strongest possible position to support your claim.
