## TL;DR

An inventory is a dated, photographed record of your property's condition at the start of a tenancy. It is your primary evidence in deposit disputes because adjudicators cannot visit the property — they decide based on paperwork alone. Without a comprehensive check-in inventory, deposit claims almost always fail, regardless of actual damage. Photograph every room, surface, appliance, and fixture; have the tenant countersign; store copies securely. A thorough inventory takes one to two hours but protects you from unfounded damage claims and ensures any legitimate claim is backed by clear, contemporaneous evidence.

## In Short

A thorough inventory is your single strongest piece of evidence in any deposit dispute. At check-in, document the condition of every room, appliance, surface, and external area with dated photographs and detailed written notes — then have the tenant countersign it. Without that baseline evidence, deposit scheme adjudicators will reject damage claims regardless of how clear the end-of-tenancy damage appears. This checklist sets out exactly what to photograph, record, and store so that your position is protected from day one.

## Why Is a Good Inventory Your Best Insurance?

Your inventory is your case in a deposit dispute — adjudicators decide based entirely on paperwork, not a site visit. When a tenancy ends and there is a dispute over the deposit, the adjudicator at the deposit protection scheme will ask one simple question: what is the evidence? If you cannot demonstrate the condition of the property at the start of the tenancy, you will struggle to prove that any damage occurred during it.

This is not a theoretical risk. The UK Government's official guidance on tenancy deposit protection, published at [www.gov.uk/tenancy-deposit-protection](https://www.gov.uk/tenancy-deposit-protection), clearly states that the burden of proof in deposit disputes falls squarely on the landlord. This position is reinforced by guidance from the three government-approved deposit protection schemes — the [Deposit Protection Service](https://www.depositprotection.com/), [MyDeposits](https://www.mydeposits.co.uk/), and the [Tenancy Deposit Scheme](https://www.tenancydepositscheme.com/) — all of which consistently emphasise that claims fail most often where the landlord's inventory is incomplete, undated, or missing entirely. A comprehensive inventory carried out on check-in day is the single most effective thing you can do to protect your position.

## What Should Your Inventory Cover?

A proper inventory is more than a list of furniture — it is a detailed record of the condition of every surface, fixture, fitting, and appliance in the property. You are creating a snapshot that can be compared against the same property at check-out, potentially years later. Each room must be documented with sufficient detail that someone reading your inventory years from now can understand exactly what condition each element was in on check-in day.

### What Should I Document Room by Room?

Every room in your property requires systematic documentation. Work through each space methodically, recording the condition of every surface and fitting. For each room, you should record:

**Walls:** Walls are prone to damage during tenancies, from scuff marks to nail holes. Record the exact colour and finish (paint, wallpaper, tiles) and photograph any existing marks, scuffs, cracks, or holes. Tenants will often try to blame you for pre-existing damage, so detailed baseline evidence is essential. Note the location of any defects using clear reference points — for example, 'two-centimetre scuff mark on the wall adjacent to the door frame at waist height.'

**Ceilings:** Ceiling damage is often overlooked at check-in but can be costly to repair. Note any stains, cracks, peeling paint, or signs of damp. Pay particular attention to bathrooms and kitchens where condensation and water damage are common.

**Floors:** The type of flooring and its condition are critical baseline facts. Record whether the floor is carpet, laminate, tile, or vinyl, and note the condition in detail, including stains, scratches, or worn areas. Carpet stains are a frequent source of dispute — photograph any existing marks clearly.

**Windows:** Window fittings are expensive to replace and easy to damage. Check the glass, frames, handles, locks, and any condensation or mould around seals. Photograph the window mechanism and test that it opens and closes smoothly. If any window locks are missing or faulty, document this clearly.

**Doors:** Note the condition of the door itself, the frame, handles, locks, and hinges. Check that every door closes properly and that locks engage smoothly. Internal door damage is common and often disputed.

**Light fittings:** Record the type of fitting in each room and confirm all bulbs are working. This protects you from claims that light fittings were broken on arrival.

**Sockets and switches:** Note the number and condition of electrical outlets and light switches in each room. Test that switches operate correctly.

**Curtains and blinds:** Record the type, colour, and condition of window coverings, including any staining or damage to hanging mechanisms. Missing curtain hooks or broken blind cords should be photographed and described.

**Built-in furniture:** Wardrobes, shelving, and cupboards — open every door and drawer and check inside. Internal damage to cupboards and wardrobes is frequently missed at check-in and becomes a source of dispute at check-out.

### What Kitchen Items Need Special Attention?

The kitchen deserves particular attention because it contains the highest concentration of items that can be damaged, stained, or go missing. Kitchen disputes are among the most common in deposit adjudications, so comprehensive documentation here is essential.

**Worktops:** Worktop damage is one of the most expensive items to claim for. Note any chips, burns, stains, or scratches in detail. Photograph the entire worktop surface from multiple angles and take close-ups of any existing defects. Even minor marks should be recorded — a small burn mark at check-in can prevent a large deduction claim later.

**Sink and taps:** Record the condition of the sink basin and taps, and check for drips or leaks. Limescale buildup, chips, and scratches should all be photographed.

**Oven, hob, and extractor:** Note whether the oven is clean or not, and record any damage such as cracked glass, broken shelves, or faulty elements. Record the brand and model. The hob should be photographed showing any existing scratches, staining, or damage. Test that the extractor fan works and note its condition.

**Fridge-freezer:** Open the fridge and freezer and photograph the condition of shelves, drawers, and door seals. Record the brand, model, and serial number. Check that the appliance is working and that the thermostat functions.

**Washing machine and dishwasher:** Record the brand, model, and serial number of both appliances. Photograph the rating plate. Check that each machine runs through a basic cycle and that door seals are intact.

**All cupboard interiors:** Open every cupboard and drawer. Photograph the interior condition. Missing shelf supports, broken drawer runners, and damage to internal surfaces are easily overlooked but commonly disputed.

### What Should I Record in the Bathroom?

Bathroom fittings are prone to damage and are expensive to replace, making detailed documentation critical.

**Bath, shower tray, and basin:** Chips, cracks, and stains in enamel or acrylic fittings are common. Photograph the entire surface of the bath, shower tray, and basin, paying particular attention to any existing damage. Check the condition of sealant around edges — poor sealant can lead to water damage and mould.

**Toilet:** Record the condition of the toilet seat, flush mechanism, and bowl. Note any staining or limescale buildup. Test that the flush works properly.

**Tiles and grouting:** Cracked tiles, missing grout, and mould on grouting are frequent issues. Photograph tile surfaces and grout lines. If mould is present, photograph it clearly — this is your evidence that the problem existed before the tenancy began.

**Extractor fan:** Check that the extractor fan works. Non-functioning ventilation can lead to damp and mould, which tenants may blame on you.

**Mirrors and cabinets:** Record the condition of mirrors and bathroom cabinets, including fixings. Check that cabinet doors open and close properly and that shelves are intact.

### What About the Garden and External Areas?

Gardens and external spaces are one of the most commonly overlooked areas in check-in inventories, yet they are also one of the most frequently disputed at check-out. If your property has a garden, patio, or any external space, document it with the same care as the interior. Without baseline evidence of the garden's condition, you will find it very difficult to claim for garden restoration costs, and adjudicators regularly reject garden-related claims for lack of check-in documentation.

**Lawn condition:** Describe whether the lawn is healthy, patchy, overgrown, or well-maintained. Photograph the entire garden from multiple angles.

**Borders, hedges, and trees:** Record whether borders are planted and weeded, whether hedges are trimmed, and the general state of any trees. If a hedge is overgrown at check-in, you cannot claim for hedge-trimming costs at check-out.

**Fencing and gates:** Check the condition of all fencing and gates. Note any broken panels, loose posts, or missing fixings. Photograph each section of the fence.

**Patio or decking:** Record the condition of hard surfaces, noting stains, moss, algae, or damage. Photograph the entire area.

**Shed or outbuilding:** If the property includes a shed, garage, or outbuilding, photograph the exterior and interior, note the contents (if any), and check the condition of the door and lock.

**Bins:** Record the number and type of bins provided (general waste, recycling, food waste). Missing or damaged bins can be a source of dispute.

## How Should I Photograph Inventory Evidence?

Written descriptions are essential, but photographs are what win deposit disputes. A well-taken set of check-in photos can be worth more than pages of written notes — provided they are dated, comprehensive, and stored somewhere you can retrieve them years later. Photographs provide objective, contemporaneous evidence that is far harder to dispute than written descriptions alone.

### Practical Rules for Effective Inventory Photos

- **Shoot wide and close.** For each room, take a wide-angle shot from the doorway showing the whole space, then close-up shots of any existing damage, marks, or wear.
- **Photograph each room from at least three angles.** A single wide shot leaves blind spots. Three angles — typically from the doorway and two opposing corners — establish the condition of every wall, floor edge, and ceiling line.
- **Use good lighting.** Open curtains, turn on lights. Dim photos obscure detail and weaken your evidence.
- **Ensure timestamps are visible.** Use a camera or phone with date and time stamping enabled in the image metadata (EXIF data). Some landlords also place a dated newspaper in the first photo of each room as additional proof.
- **Photograph every wall.** Stand in the centre of each room and photograph all four walls systematically.
- **Capture existing damage in detail.** Scratches on worktops, chips on bath enamel, stains on carpet — photograph everything, no matter how minor. If it is not photographed, it does not exist for dispute purposes.
- **Include appliance serial numbers.** Take a close-up of the rating plate on every appliance, showing the brand, model, and serial number.
- **Do not forget ceilings.** Especially in bathrooms and kitchens where staining or damp is common.
- **Take a minimum of 50 photos for a one-bedroom flat, and 80–100 for a three-bedroom house.** Volume matters: under-documenting is the most common reason photo evidence fails in disputes.

<Callout variant="info">Store your photos in a dedicated folder named with the property address and check-in date. Back them up to cloud storage immediately. Photos stored only on a phone that gets lost or replaced are no use to anyone.</Callout>

## What Meter Readings Should I Take?

On check-in day, read every utility meter at the property and record the readings in your inventory. This protects both you and the tenant — it ensures the tenant is only billed for energy used during their tenancy, and it avoids disputes with utility companies over who is responsible for consumption before or after the tenancy.

- **Gas meter** — note the reading and meter serial number
- **Electricity meter** — note the reading (day and night if it is an Economy 7 meter) and serial number
- **Water meter** — if the property has one, record the reading

Photograph each meter showing the reading clearly. This creates an unambiguous record of the starting point for utility usage.

## What Keys and Security Items Should I Document?

Document every key and security device handed to the tenant. Lost keys are a legitimate deduction from the deposit, but only if you can prove how many were provided at the start. Record the following:

- Number of front door keys
- Number of back door keys
- Window keys
- Garage or shed keys
- Fob or code for communal entry systems
- Alarm codes and instructions

Record the number of each type of key in the inventory and have the tenant acknowledge receipt in writing. If keys go missing at the end of the tenancy, you will have clear evidence of what was provided.

## What Is the Difference Between a Schedule of Condition and a Full Inventory?

You may come across two terms used somewhat interchangeably, but they serve different purposes and it is important to understand which you need.

A **full inventory** lists every item in the property along with its condition. This is appropriate for furnished or part-furnished lettings where you are providing furniture, kitchenware, appliances, and other items that the tenant will use.

A **schedule of condition** records the state of the property's fixtures, fittings, and surfaces without listing individual moveable items. This is more suitable for unfurnished lettings where you are providing the bare property.

For most small landlords letting a furnished or part-furnished property, a full inventory is the safer choice. If you are letting unfurnished, a schedule of condition is usually sufficient — but make sure it is detailed enough to cover every surface and fitting.

## How Do I Get the Tenant to Sign the Inventory?

Once the inventory is complete, provide a copy to the tenant and ask them to review and countersign it. This step is important for two reasons.

First, a countersigned inventory carries significantly more weight in a deposit dispute. It demonstrates that the tenant agreed with your assessment of the property's condition at the start of the tenancy.

Second, it gives the tenant a fair opportunity to note anything they disagree with or any damage you may have missed. The Deposit Protection Service, MyDeposits, and the Tenancy Deposit Scheme all recommend giving the tenant seven days to review the inventory and raise any amendments.

<PullQuote>A signed inventory is a mutual agreement about the starting condition of the property. Without it, the tenant can simply dispute your version of events.</PullQuote>

If the tenant refuses to sign, note that refusal in writing and send them a copy of the inventory by email so you have a record of delivery. An unsigned inventory is still valid evidence — it simply carries less weight than one the tenant has acknowledged.

## Should I Use a Digital or Paper Inventory?

There is no legal requirement to use a particular format. What matters is that the inventory is detailed, dated, and supported by photographs.

**Paper inventories** work perfectly well. Print two copies, walk through the property with the tenant, both sign, each keep a copy. The disadvantage is that paper can be lost, and linking printed photos to specific rooms can be cumbersome.

**Digital inventories** are increasingly common and offer practical advantages. You can embed photographs directly alongside written descriptions, timestamp everything automatically, and store copies securely in the cloud. Several apps and software tools are designed specifically for property inventories.

**Professional inventory services** are another option worth considering. An independent inventory clerk will carry out the check-in, produce a detailed report with photographs, and provide an impartial record. This typically costs between £80 and £200 depending on the size of the property. The advantage is that the report comes from a neutral third party, which can carry additional weight in disputes. If you are [managing your property without a letting agent](/blog/managing-rental-property-without-letting-agent), this is one task where paying a professional can be a sound investment.

## What Common Mistakes Should I Avoid?

Even experienced landlords can undermine their own evidence by overlooking the basics. Here are the most frequent errors and how to avoid them:

- **Not photographing the garden.** As noted above, this is one of the most frequent omissions and one of the hardest to recover from in a dispute.
- **Not recording appliance serial numbers.** If an appliance is swapped or damaged during the tenancy, you need to prove what was originally provided.
- **Not dating photographs.** Photos without metadata or a visible date reference have limited evidential value. An adjudicator needs to be satisfied that the photos were taken at the start of the tenancy, not at some other time.
- **Being too vague.**
