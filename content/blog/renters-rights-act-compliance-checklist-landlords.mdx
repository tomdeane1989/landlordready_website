## Your Complete Landlord Compliance Checklist for the Renters' Rights Act

**Executive Summary:** The Renters' Rights Act 2025 ([full text](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents)) takes effect on 1 May 2026 and requires every private landlord in England to meet new obligations across six core areas: safety certificates, tenancy structure changes (end of Section 21 and fixed terms), rent increase controls, mandatory registration (Property Portal and PRS Ombudsman), upgraded property standards (Decent Homes and Awaab's Law), and comprehensive documentation. Non-compliance carries financial penalties up to £30,000 per offence, loss of possession rights in court, and potential criminal liability—making compliance urgent for all private landlords in England. This checklist provides every action item, deadline, and practical step you must complete before the 1 May 2026 deadline.

## 1. Safety Certificates: Get Every Certificate Current and Compliant

**Private landlords must hold current Gas Safety (CP12), Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR), Energy Performance Certificate (EPC), and smoke/CO alarm compliance by 1 May 2026 or face loss of possession rights and financial penalties.** Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, all safety documentation must be valid and uploaded to the new Property Portal—failure to do so will prevent you from taking enforcement action against tenants.

### Gas Safety Certificate (CP12)

- [ ] Obtain a valid [gas safety certificate](/blog/gas-safety-certificate-landlord-guide-2026) from a [Gas Safe registered engineer](https://www.gassaferegister.co.uk/) and upload it to the Property Portal by 1 May 2026. Absence blocks possession proceedings under the Act.
- [ ] Ensure the certificate is renewed annually — no exceptions
- [ ] Provide a copy to your tenant within 28 days of the check
- [ ] Upload the current certificate to the Property Portal

<Callout variant="warning">If your gas safety certificate lapses, you will not be able to serve a valid possession notice under the reformed grounds. Get this booked well in advance of the expiry date — do not leave it until the last week.</Callout>

### Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR)

- [ ] Commission a satisfactory [EICR from a qualified electrician](/blog/electrical-safety-certificate-landlord-requirements) and upload it to the Property Portal by 1 May 2026. Absence blocks possession proceedings.
- [ ] Address any C1 (danger present) or C2 (potentially dangerous) issues immediately
- [ ] Ensure the report is renewed at least every five years
- [ ] Provide a copy to your tenant before they move in, or within 28 days of the inspection
- [ ] Upload to the Property Portal

### Energy Performance Certificate (EPC)

- [ ] Obtain a valid [EPC for each rental property](/blog/epc-rating-requirements-rental-property-2026) and confirm the rating meets the current minimum requirement (E or above) by 1 May 2026
- [ ] Confirm the rating meets the current minimum requirement (E or above)
- [ ] Plan ahead for the expected tightening to a minimum C rating in future years
- [ ] Upload to the Property Portal

<Callout variant="info">EPC requirements are expected to tighten further. If your property currently sits at a D or E rating, now is the time to investigate [energy efficiency improvements](/blog/energy-efficiency-improvements-rental-property) so you are not caught out by future changes.</Callout>

### Smoke and Carbon Monoxide Alarms

- [ ] Install [smoke alarms on every storey](/blog/smoke-carbon-monoxide-alarm-regulations-landlords) of the property and test all alarms at the start of each new tenancy—failure to comply carries financial penalties and blocks possession rights
- [ ] Install carbon monoxide alarms in any room with a fixed combustion appliance (excluding gas cookers)
- [ ] Test all alarms at the start of each new tenancy
- [ ] Document compliance and retain records
- [ ] Confirm compliance on the Property Portal

### Fire Safety

- [ ] Review your obligations under [fire safety regulations](/blog/fire-safety-regulations-rental-property), particularly if you let an HMO or a property in a building with common areas, and document all fire safety measures by 1 May 2026
- [ ] Ensure fire escape routes are clear and documented
- [ ] Provide fire safety information to tenants where required
- [ ] Carry out a fire risk assessment if you manage an HMO or multi-occupancy building
- [ ] Install fire doors, extinguishers, and emergency lighting where required by regulations
- [ ] Keep records of all fire safety inspections and maintenance

### Legionella Risk Assessment

- [ ] Carry out a [legionella risk assessment](/blog/legionella-risk-assessment-landlords) for the property and implement control measures by 1 May 2026
- [ ] Implement any control measures identified by the assessment
- [ ] Review the assessment periodically, especially after any plumbing changes
- [ ] Document all assessments and remedial actions taken

## 2. Tenancy Changes: Prepare for the End of Section 21 and Fixed Terms

**Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, all assured tenancies become periodic from 1 May 2026, and Section 21 no-fault evictions are abolished—landlords must now use reformed Section 8 grounds for possession, which require valid compliance documentation.** This is the single biggest structural change, and your tenancy management processes need to be updated accordingly.

### Section 21 Abolition

- [ ] Understand that [Section 21 notices can no longer be served](/blog/how-to-end-tenancy-after-section-21-abolished) from 1 May 2026 under the Renters' Rights Act 2025
- [ ] If you have a Section 21 notice already served, take legal advice on whether it will remain valid
- [ ] Familiarise yourself with the reformed Section 8 grounds for possession
- [ ] Update any template letters or processes that reference Section 21

<Callout variant="warning">Any Section 21 notice that has not resulted in a court order by 1 May 2026 may become invalid. If you are mid-process, speak to a solicitor now — not in April.</Callout>

### Periodic Tenancies

- [ ] Understand that all new tenancies created after 1 May 2026 will be [periodic from day one](/blog/periodic-tenancy-explained-landlords) under the Act—fixed-term tenancies are no longer permitted
- [ ] Existing fixed-term tenancies will convert to periodic once they expire
- [ ] Update your tenancy agreement templates to reflect the periodic structure
- [ ] Accept that tenants can give two months' notice to leave at any time

### Possession Grounds

- [ ] Learn the mandatory grounds under the reformed Section 8 (sale, landlord moving in, redevelopment, serious rent arrears) as specified in the Renters' Rights Act 2025
- [ ] Understand the 12-month protected period at the start of a tenancy for Grounds 1 and 1A
- [ ] Note the four-month notice period required for most mandatory grounds
- [ ] Keep records that demonstrate compliance, as this will be essential for any court proceedings

### Pet Requests

- [ ] Prepare for the new right for tenants to [request permission to keep a pet](/blog/tenant-pet-requests-new-rules-landlords) under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, which requires landlords to respond within a reasonable timeframe
- [ ] Understand the grounds on which you can reasonably refuse
- [ ] Consider whether your insurance covers pet damage, and explore pet damage insurance if needed
- [ ] Draft a clear pet policy for your tenancy documentation

### Notice Requirements and Deposit Procedures

- [ ] Ensure all notice periods comply with the new minimums: four months for most landlord possession grounds, two months for tenant notice to quit
- [ ] Update deposit protection procedures to align with the Act's requirements—deposits must be protected within 30 days and prescribed information served
- [ ] Provide tenants with written confirmation of deposit protection scheme details
- [ ] Review and update your check-out procedure to ensure fair deposit deductions that comply with the Act

## 3. Rent Rules: Follow the New Rent Increase Framework

**Under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, all rent increases must follow the formal Section 13 notice procedure—limited to once per year with two months' notice—and tenants have the right to challenge any increase at the First-tier Tribunal.** Informal rent rises and break-clause-linked increases are no longer permitted.

### Section 13 Notices

- [ ] All rent increases must now follow the [Section 13 notice procedure](/blog/section-13-rent-increase-notice-new-rules) as specified in the Renters' Rights Act 2025
- [ ] Increases are limited to once per year under the Act
- [ ] The notice period is two months
- [ ] Tenants have the right to challenge any increase at the First-tier Tribunal
- [ ] The Tribunal will assess whether the proposed rent is in line with open market rates

<PullQuote>Rent increases are not banned under the Renters' Rights Act — but they must follow a formal process and be defensible at tribunal. Evidence of comparable market rents is your best protection.</PullQuote>

### Practical Steps for Rent Compliance

- [ ] Keep records of comparable local rents to justify any increase—the Tribunal will require evidence that your proposed rent aligns with market rates
- [ ] Use the correct Section 13 notice form — do not rely on informal letters or emails
- [ ] Allow the full two-month notice period before the increase takes effect
- [ ] Remove any rent review clauses from your tenancy agreements that conflict with the new regime

### Payment Methods and Transparency

- [ ] Ensure you offer at least one fee-free rent payment method (e.g., bank transfer or standing order)—the Act prohibits charging tenants for making rent payments
- [ ] Provide clear written information about acceptable payment methods at the start of the tenancy
- [ ] Do not impose payment surcharges or administrative fees for rent collection
- [ ] Keep clear records of all rent payments received and dates

## 4. Registration and Ombudsman: Mandatory Sign-Up Before Day One

**The Renters' Rights Act 2025 requires every private landlord in England to register on the Property Portal and join the PRS Ombudsman scheme by 1 May 2026—failure to comply is a criminal offence and will prevent you from taking possession proceedings in court.**

### Property Portal Registration

- [ ] [Register on the Property Portal](/blog/landlord-registration-property-portal-explained) as soon as registration opens—the Renters' Rights Act 2025 makes this mandatory for all private landlords
- [ ] Add every rental property you own to the portal
- [ ] Upload all compliance documents (gas, electrical, EPC, deposit protection, alarms)
- [ ] Keep records updated whenever certificates are renewed or tenancies change
- [ ] Check that your registration is complete — incomplete entries may count as non-compliance

<Callout variant="info">The Property Portal is not optional. Failure to register is a criminal offence under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, and an unregistered landlord will face severe difficulties in pursuing possession through the courts. Treat this as a priority, not an afterthought.</Callout>

### PRS Ombudsman Membership

- [ ] Join the [Private Rented Sector Ombudsman scheme](/blog/prs-ombudsman-landlord-membership-requirements) before 1 May 2026—the Act mandates membership for all private landlords in England
- [ ] Understand that membership is mandatory for all private landlords in England — not just agents
- [ ] Budget for the annual membership fee
- [ ] Familiarise yourself with the complaints process so you can respond appropriately if a tenant raises a dispute
- [ ] Confirm your membership details on the Property Portal

### Complaint Procedures

- [ ] Establish a clear internal complaints procedure for tenants—the Ombudsman expects landlords to attempt resolution before escalation
- [ ] Respond to tenant complaints in writing within 10 working days
- [ ] Keep records of all complaints received and your responses
- [ ] Understand the Ombudsman's powers to award compensation if a complaint is upheld
- [ ] Display your Ombudsman membership details clearly in all tenant communications

## 5. Property Standards: Meet the Decent Homes Standard and Awaab's Law

**The Renters' Rights Act 2025 extends the Decent Homes Standard and Awaab's Law (damp and mould response requirements) to all private rental properties in England—landlords must ensure properties are free from Category 1 hazards and respond to damp/mould reports within prescribed timeframes or face financial penalties and loss of possession rights.**

### Decent Homes Standard

- [ ] Review the [Decent Homes Standard requirements](/blog/decent-homes-standard-private-landlords-england) as they apply to private rentals under the Act—properties must be free from serious hazards and meet minimum standards for repair, facilities, and thermal comfort
- [ ] Ensure the property is free from serious hazards under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS)
- [ ] Confirm the property is in a reasonable state of repair
- [ ] Check that kitchens, bathrooms, and heating systems meet the minimum standards
- [ ] Address any Category 1 hazards immediately

### Awaab's Law — Damp and Mould

- [ ] Understand your obligations under [Awaab's Law for private landlords](/blog/awaabs-law-private-landlords-damp-mould)—the Act requires landlords to investigate damp and mould reports within 14 days and carry out remedial work within specified timeframes
- [ ] Respond to tenant reports of damp and mould within the prescribed timeframes
- [ ] Investigate the root cause — not just the surface symptoms
- [ ] Carry out repairs promptly and document every step
- [ ] Never blame tenants for condensation without proper investigation

<Callout variant="warning">Awaab's Law was named after the death of a two-year-old boy from prolonged mould exposure. Courts and tribunals will take damp and mould complaints extremely seriously. A dismissive response is not an option.</Callout>

### Standards Enforcement and Remediation Deadlines

- [ ] Carry out a self-assessment of your property against the Decent Homes Standard by 1 April 2026
- [ ] Create a remediation plan for any shortfalls identified, with target completion dates
- [ ] Budget for necessary works—upgrades to kitchens, bathrooms, heating systems, and insulation may be required
- [ ] Understand that local authorities can issue improvement notices under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System—failure to comply carries financial penalties up to £30,000
- [ ] Keep detailed records of all maintenance, repairs, and upgrades carried out

### Right to Rent Checks

- [ ] Continue carrying out [right to rent checks](/blog/right-to-rent-checks-landlord-guide) on all prospective tenants before granting a tenancy—this obligation remains unchanged under the Renters' Rights Act 2025
- [ ] Use the Home Office online checking service where possible
- [ ] Keep copies of documents for the required retention period
- [ ] Carry out follow-up checks where a tenant has time-limited permission to remain in the UK

## 6. Documentation: Keep Your Records Airtight

**The Renters' Rights Act 2025 compliance framework depends on documentation—landlords must provide written tenancy agreements with prescribed information, protect deposits within 30 days, and maintain comprehensive records of all compliance activities or face inability to enforce tenancy terms in court.**

### Tenancy Agreements

- [ ] Update your tenancy agreement template to remove references to fixed terms and Section 21—the Act mandates periodic tenancies only
- [ ] Include the prescribed information required under the new regime (landlord name and address, Ombudsman details, deposit protection information)
- [ ] Ensure the agreement reflects the periodic tenancy structure
- [ ] Provide the tenant with a written agreement before the tenancy begins

### Deposit Protection

- [ ] Protect every deposit with an [approved tenancy deposit scheme](/blog/tenant-deposit-protection-rules-landlords) ([government guidance](https://www.gov.uk/deposit-protection-schemes-and-landlords)) within 30 days—the Renters' Rights Act 2025 maintains this requirement and failure to comply blocks possession proceedings
- [ ] Serve the prescribed information on the tenant
- [ ] Keep proof of protection and service
- [ ] Upload deposit protection details to the Property Portal

### Inventories and Check-In Documentation

- [ ] Prepare a comprehensive inventory with photographs for every property—this is essential for defending deposit deductions at the end of a tenancy
- [ ] Carry out a detailed check-in inspection with the tenant present
- [ ] Provide the tenant with a copy of the inventory and check-in report within seven days
- [ ] Keep signed acknowledgement from the tenant that they have received and reviewed the inventory

### Record Keeping

- [ ] Maintain a compliance file for each rental property—courts and tribunals will require evidence of compliance when considering possession proceedings
- [ ] Store copies of all certificates, notices, and correspondence
- [ ] Keep a log of maintenance requests and how they were resolved
- [ ] Record all rent increases, including the Section 13 notice and evidence used
- [ ] Retain right to rent check documents for the required period

<PullQuote>The landlords who will navigate the Renters' Rights Act most successfully are those who treat compliance as an ongoing system — not a one-off exercise in May 2026.</PullQuote>

## What Happens If You Are Not Compliant by 1 May 2026?

The consequences of non-compliance are serious. According to the Renters' Rights Act 2025, depending on the obligation you have missed, you could face:

- **Financial penalties** imposed by local authorities, potentially up to £30,000 per offence for civil penalties under the Act
- **Criminal prosecution** for failing to register on the Property Portal or join the Ombudsman
- **Inability to recover possession** — courts may refuse to grant possession orders if your compliance records are incomplete
- **Rent repayment orders** — tenants may apply for repayment of rent if you have committed certain offences
- **Ombudsman rulings** requiring you to pay compensation to tenants

<Callout variant="warning">The Renters' Rights Act gives local authorities enhanced enforcement powers. Budget pressures may mean enforcement is uneven at first, but do not gamble on being overlooked. One tenant complaint is all it takes to trigger an investigation.</Callout>

## Your Timeline: What to Do Between Now and 1 May 2026

You have weeks, not months. Here is a practical priority order:

1. **This week:** Audit every safety certificate. Book renewals for anything expiring before or shortly after 1 May 2026.
2. **By end of March:** Join the PRS Ombudsman scheme. Update your tenancy agreement templates.
3. **By mid-April:** Register on the Property Portal as soon as it opens. Upload all compliance documents.
4. **By end of April:** Brief any letting agents or property managers on the changes. Confirm that every property in your portfolio is covered.
5. **1 May 2026 onwards:** Operate under the new regime. Section 21 is gone. Periodic tenancies are the standard. Compliance is not optional.
