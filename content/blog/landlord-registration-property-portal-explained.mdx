*Published: 15 March 2026 | Last updated: 4 June 2026*

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 introduces a mandatory Property Portal — a centralised digital database where every private landlord in England must register themselves and their rental properties by 1 May 2026. All private landlords, including single-property owners and portfolio landlords, must register on the portal and upload compliance documents (gas safety certificates, EICRs, EPCs, and deposit protection details). According to the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents), your portal registration will be checked by courts when processing possession orders, and failure to register is a criminal offence carrying financial penalties. The portal provides transparency for tenants, enforcement data for local authorities, and a single compliance hub for landlords who maintain their properties properly.

## A Single Place for Landlord Compliance

One of the most significant practical changes under the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents) is the creation of a new **Property Portal** — a centralised digital database where every private landlord in England must register themselves and their rental properties. Think of it as HMRC's Self Assessment, but for lettings compliance.

The portal is designed to bring transparency and accountability to the private rented sector. For tenants, it provides a way to verify that a landlord is legitimate and compliant. For landlords, it creates a single place to demonstrate that your properties meet the required standards. For local authorities, it provides the data they need to enforce the law efficiently.

## Who Needs to Register?

According to the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents), every private landlord who lets residential property in England under an assured tenancy will need to register on the Property Portal. The [government's guidance on renting out a property](https://www.gov.uk/renting-out-a-property) provides an overview of landlord obligations. This includes:

- Landlords with a single buy-to-let property
- Portfolio landlords with multiple properties
- Landlords who use a letting agent to manage their properties (the obligation falls on the landlord, not the agent)

<Callout variant="warning">Using a managing agent does not exempt you from registration. The legal duty sits with the landlord. Your agent may help you with the process, but you are responsible for ensuring it is done.</Callout>

## What Will You Need to Provide?

While the final technical specification is still being developed (as of June 2026), the government has indicated through [guidance published on gov.uk](https://www.gov.uk/renting-out-a-property) that the portal will require landlords to upload and maintain:

### Landlord Details

- Full legal name and correspondence address
- Contact details (email, telephone)
- Confirmation of membership of the Private Rented Sector Ombudsman scheme

### Property Details

- Address of each rental property
- Type of property (house, flat, HMO, etc.)
- Number of bedrooms and occupants

### Compliance Documents

- **[Gas safety certificate](/blog/gas-safety-certificate-landlord-guide-2026)** — current and valid
- **[Electrical Installation Condition Report (EICR)](/blog/electrical-safety-certificate-landlord-requirements)** — current and satisfactory
- **[Energy Performance Certificate (EPC)](/blog/epc-rating-requirements-rental-property-2026)** — current and meeting the minimum rating
- **[Smoke and carbon monoxide alarm compliance](/blog/smoke-carbon-monoxide-alarm-regulations-landlords)** — confirmation that alarms are installed as required
- **[Deposit protection details](/blog/tenant-deposit-protection-rules-landlords)** — which scheme and the certificate reference

## Why the Portal Matters

### For Possession Proceedings

According to the Renters' Rights Act 2025, your ability to [pursue possession through the courts](/blog/how-to-end-tenancy-after-section-21-abolished) will depend on your portal registration being up to date. If you are not registered, or your compliance documents have lapsed, the court may refuse to grant a possession order — or the tenant may use your non-registration as a defence.

### For Avoiding Penalties

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 establishes that failing to register, providing false information, or failing to keep records up to date will be a criminal offence carrying a financial penalty. The exact penalty levels are being set through secondary legislation, but the government has made clear that enforcement will be robust.

### For Tenant Relations

Tenants will be able to check the portal to verify their landlord's registration and compliance status, complementing the existing [government guidance on private renting rights](https://www.gov.uk/private-renting). A landlord who is fully registered with all documents in order sends a clear signal of professionalism. A landlord who is absent from the portal — or whose records show lapsed certificates — will face difficult questions.

<Callout variant="tip">Getting your portal registration right from day one establishes you as a compliant, professional landlord. It is a simple step that protects your reputation and your legal position.</Callout>

## The Ombudsman Requirement

The Property Portal is closely linked to the new **Private Rented Sector Ombudsman**. According to government guidance, all private landlords must join the ombudsman scheme, and your membership details must be recorded on the portal.

The ombudsman will handle tenant complaints that cannot be resolved directly between landlord and tenant. It can order compensation, require you to take specific action (such as carrying out repairs), and issue formal findings against you. While this may feel like another layer of regulation, it also provides a structured process for resolving disputes without costly court proceedings.

## How the Portal Interacts with Local Authorities

Local councils currently struggle to identify and monitor private landlords — particularly those who operate without letting agents. The Property Portal changes this by giving local authorities direct access to a database of every registered landlord and property in their area.

This means:

- Councils can proactively check compliance rather than waiting for complaints
- Targeted enforcement becomes easier and more efficient
- Landlords who are already compliant will face less disruption, not more

## Preparing for the Portal Now

Even though the portal has not yet launched, there is plenty you can do to prepare:

- **Gather your compliance documents.** Collect digital copies of your gas safety certificate, EICR, EPC, and deposit protection certificates for every property
- **Check expiry dates.** If any certificate is due to expire in the next six months, renew it now rather than scrambling later
- **Confirm your ombudsman membership.** Make sure you know which scheme you are joining and that your membership is active
- **Verify your EPC ratings.** If any property is close to or below the minimum standard, plan improvements now
- **[Organise your records digitally.](/blog/storing-tenancy-documents-securely)** The portal will be a digital system, so having your documents in digital format — ideally in a cloud-based system — will make registration smoother

<Callout variant="warning">Do not wait for the portal to launch before getting your compliance in order. The portal will expose any gaps immediately, and you do not want to be scrambling to book electricians and gas engineers when every other landlord is doing the same thing.</Callout>

## A Positive Change for Good Landlords

If you are already maintaining your properties properly and keeping your certificates up to date, the Property Portal should hold no fear. It is essentially a digital reflection of what you are already doing. The landlords who will find it burdensome are those who have been cutting corners — and the portal is specifically designed to bring them into line.

For diligent private landlords in England, the portal may actually be beneficial. It levels the playing field by ensuring that every landlord meets the same standards, which reduces unfair competition from non-compliant operators and raises the overall reputation of the private rented sector.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When exactly must I register on the Property Portal?**
According to the Renters' Rights Act 2025, all private landlords must register on the Property Portal by 1 May 2026. This deadline applies whether you have one property or a portfolio, and whether you manage the property yourself or use a letting agent.

**What happens if I don't register by 1 May 2026?**
Failure to register is a criminal offence under the Renters' Rights Act 2025 and carries financial penalties. Additionally, courts may refuse to grant possession orders if you are not registered, meaning you will be unable to regain possession of your property through legal channels until you comply.

**Can my letting agent register on my behalf?**
No. While your letting agent can assist with the registration process, the legal obligation to register sits with you as the landlord. You must ensure the registration is completed in your name and that all information is accurate and up to date.

**What if I don't have all the compliance documents yet?**
You should obtain all required compliance documents before registering on the portal. This includes a valid gas safety certificate, EICR, EPC, smoke and carbon monoxide alarm confirmation, and deposit protection details. Registering without these documents will leave your registration incomplete and may result in penalties.

**Is there a fee to register on the Property Portal?**
The government has not yet confirmed whether there will be a registration fee. Details about any fees, as well as the full technical specification of the portal, are expected to be announced through secondary legislation before the portal launches.
