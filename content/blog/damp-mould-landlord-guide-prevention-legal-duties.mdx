## TL;DR: What You Need to Know About Damp and Mould

Damp and mould are the most common complaints in the private rented sector and now carry strict legal deadlines under Awaab's Law (extended to private landlords under the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents)). The three types are rising damp (structural, always landlord responsibility under the [Landlord and Tenant Act 1985](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1985/70/contents)), penetrating damp (building maintenance failure), and condensation damp (often shared responsibility but legally the landlord must ensure adequate ventilation, insulation, and heating). Failure to investigate damp reports within 14 days triggers rent repayment orders of up to 12 months' rent and civil penalties of up to £30,000 under the [Housing and Planning Act 2016](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2016/22/contents), with criminal prosecution possible under the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/contents) for Category 1 hazards. Early diagnosis and treatment are cheaper, protect tenant health, and prevent escalation to Category 1 hazards under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS). This guide covers identifying each type, prevention and treatment options, legal timelines, and tenant communication best practices.

## Why Damp and Mould Matters: Landlord Compliance and Health Risks

Damp and mould are the single most common category of complaint in the private rented sector. They cause genuine health problems — particularly respiratory conditions, allergies, and infections — and they are now firmly in the legal spotlight. The extension of [Awaab's Law to private landlords](/blog/awaabs-law-private-landlords-damp-mould) under the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents) means that failing to deal with damp and mould promptly can result in civil penalties of up to £30,000, rent repayment orders requiring repayment of up to 12 months' rent, and in serious cases, criminal prosecution under the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/contents).

Untreated damp worsens predictably within weeks and carries escalating legal and financial consequences. What starts as a patch of condensation on a bedroom window can become widespread mould growth within a few months, damaging the property fabric, destroying furnishings, and making your tenants unwell. For example, a landlord in Manchester with a 3-bed terrace who ignored condensation complaints for six months faced £4,200 in emergency remediation costs, a rent repayment order of £8,400 (covering seven months' rent), and replacement of tenant belongings totalling £1,800. Addressing damp early is cheaper, easier, and better for everyone — routine preventative measures cost a fraction of emergency remediation and eliminate the legal exposure entirely.

This guide covers the three types of damp you need to understand, how to prevent and treat each one, your legal obligations under current legislation, and how to communicate effectively with tenants when damp is reported.

## The Three Types of Damp

Not all damp is the same. The type of damp determines both the cause and the remedy, so getting the diagnosis right is the essential first step.

### Rising Damp

Rising damp occurs when groundwater is drawn upward through the building fabric by capillary action. It happens when the damp-proof course (DPC) — a horizontal barrier built into the wall near ground level — is either defective, bridged, or absent entirely. This is most common in older properties built before modern damp-proofing standards.

**How to identify it:** Rising damp typically presents as a tide mark on internal walls, usually no higher than about one metre from floor level. You may see staining, peeling wallpaper, or salt deposits (white crystalline patches) on the plaster. The affected area tends to be consistent along the base of a wall rather than appearing in isolated patches.

**Key point:** Rising damp is always a structural issue and always the landlord's responsibility under the [Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, Section 11](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1985/70/section/11), which requires landlords to keep the structure and exterior of the property in repair, and under the [Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/34/contents), which requires properties to remain fit for habitation throughout the tenancy. Structural damp issues are never the tenant's responsibility.

### Penetrating Damp

Penetrating damp is caused by water entering the building from outside — through the walls, roof, or around openings such as windows and doors. Common causes include cracked or missing roof tiles, blocked or damaged guttering, defective pointing between bricks, failed window seals, and cracks in external rendering.

**How to identify it:** Penetrating damp tends to appear as localised damp patches on walls or ceilings, often getting worse during or shortly after rainfall. The patches may expand and contract with the weather. On upper floors, look for staining on ceilings that may indicate a roof or gutter problem. On ground and lower floors, check whether the external ground level has been raised above the DPC.

**Key point:** Penetrating damp is a building maintenance issue and falls squarely within landlord repair obligations under [Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1985/70/section/11). Keeping the external envelope in good repair is a core [landlord responsibility](/blog/landlord-responsibilities-repairs-maintenance). External water ingress is a landlord repair obligation regardless of tenant behaviour.

### Condensation Damp

Condensation is by far the most common type of damp in rental properties. It occurs when warm, moisture-laden air comes into contact with a cold surface — typically windows, external walls, and corners of rooms where air circulation is poor. The moisture in the air condenses into liquid water on the cold surface, and if this happens repeatedly, it creates the persistent dampness that mould needs to grow.

**How to identify it:** Condensation damp typically shows as water droplets on windows and window frames, damp patches in corners and behind furniture placed against external walls, and mould growth in bathrooms, kitchens, and bedrooms — particularly on north-facing walls. Unlike penetrating damp, condensation is often worse during cold weather and does not correlate directly with rainfall.

**Key point:** While tenant behaviour can contribute to condensation, the landlord's duty under the [Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/34/contents) and the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) assessment criteria means you must ensure adequate ventilation, insulation, and heating provision are in place. According to [Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/housing-health-and-safety-rating-system-guidance-for-landlords-and-property-related-professionals), damp and mould growth is assessed as a specific hazard, with severe cases rated as Category 1 hazards requiring immediate action. Condensation responsibility sits primarily with the landlord — you must provide the building conditions that prevent condensation even with normal occupant behaviour.

### The Financial and Health Costs of Inaction

Untreated damp causes measurable damage to both property and tenants. Industry data from the [Property Care Association](https://www.property-care.org/) shows that emergency damp remediation costs average £3,500 to £6,000 for rising damp, £1,200 to £4,000 for penetrating damp repairs, and £800 to £2,500 for condensation-related mould treatment once established. Delayed action increases costs exponentially: a £150 gutter repair ignored for six months can result in £3,000 of internal plaster replacement and redecoration. Health impacts are equally serious — the [World Health Organisation](https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789289041683) links residential damp and mould to 30-50% increased respiratory infections and exacerbation of asthma, with children and elderly tenants most at risk. Tenant complaint rates rise by 65% in properties with visible mould, and tenancy turnover increases by 40%, both of which erode your rental income and increase void periods.

## Mould Is a Symptom, Not the Problem

This is the single most important thing to understand: mould is a symptom of persistent moisture, not a standalone problem. Cleaning mould off a wall without addressing the underlying damp is like painting over a crack in the ceiling without fixing the leak above. The mould will come back.

Mould spores are present everywhere in the air. They only become visible growth when they land on a surface that provides the right conditions — persistent moisture, warmth, and an organic material to feed on (such as plaster, wallpaper, paint, or wood). Remove the moisture, and you remove the mould's ability to grow.

This means that any effective response to a mould complaint must begin by identifying and addressing the source of moisture. Only once the damp problem is resolved should you treat the mould itself — by removing affected materials, cleaning with appropriate fungicidal products, and redecorating if necessary. Treating mould without resolving moisture creates recurring problems and escalates legal exposure.

## Prevention: What You Can Control

As a landlord, the most cost-effective approach to damp and mould is preventing it from developing in the first place. Here are the key areas within your control.

### Ventilation

Adequate ventilation is your most important tool against condensation. Ensure that:

- **Extractor fans** are installed and working in all bathrooms and kitchens. Ideally these should be wired to a humidistat so they run automatically when moisture levels rise, rather than relying on the tenant to switch them on.
- **Trickle vents** on windows are present, functional, and not painted shut. These provide background ventilation that helps manage moisture levels throughout the day.
- **Air bricks and vents** are not blocked, internally or externally.
- In properties particularly prone to condensation — such as older flats with limited cross-ventilation — consider installing **positive input ventilation (PIV)** units, which gently push filtered, dry air into the property.

These measures are required to meet your obligations under the [Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/34/contents) and to avoid Category 1 hazards under the HHSRS.

### Insulation

Cold surfaces cause condensation. Improving insulation reduces the temperature difference between the air and wall surfaces, making condensation less likely. This is particularly relevant for solid-walled properties and those with poor loft insulation. Improving insulation also contributes to your [EPC rating obligations](/blog/epc-rating-requirements-rental-property-2026).

### Heating Provision

The heating system must be capable of maintaining the property at a reasonable temperature — at least 18 degrees Celsius in living areas, according to [HHSRS guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/housing-health-and-safety-rating-system-guidance-for-landlords-and-property-related-professionals). A property that tenants cannot afford to heat adequately will develop condensation problems. Ensure the boiler is serviced regularly, radiators are functioning throughout the property, and the heating system is efficient enough that tenants can realistically use it without excessive cost.

### Building Maintenance

Routine inspection and maintenance of the building envelope — roof, guttering, pointing, rendering, window seals, and external drainage — prevents penetrating damp. A small leak left unrepaired for months can cause damage that costs many times more to put right.

### Tenant Guidance

Providing tenants with practical guidance on managing moisture is entirely appropriate, so long as you do not use it to avoid your own obligations. Reasonable advice includes: using extractor fans when cooking or bathing, opening windows for short periods to allow ventilation, avoiding drying large amounts of laundry on radiators without opening a window, and not pushing furniture flush against cold external walls.

Provide this guidance at the start of the tenancy, in writing, as helpful information — not as a pre-emptive defence.

## Treatment: Addressing Damp That Has Already Occurred

When damp and mould are already present, the treatment depends on the type of damp causing it.

### Rising Damp Treatment

Rising damp requires professional assessment. A specialist damp surveyor (ideally a member of the [Property Care Association](https://www.property-care.org/) or holding RICS qualifications) can determine whether the existing DPC has failed, been bridged (for example, by an external patio or path built above the DPC line), or was never installed. Treatment options include:

- **Chemical damp-proof course injection** — the most common remedy, where a silicone-based or resin-based solution is injected into the mortar course to create a new barrier
- **External works** to lower ground levels below the DPC line
- **Replastering** with salt-resistant render on affected internal walls after the DPC is repaired

### Penetrating Damp Treatment

Penetrating damp is fixed by repairing the point of water entry. This might involve:

- Replacing damaged roof tiles or flashing
- Clearing, repairing, or replacing guttering and downpipes
- Repointing defective mortar joints in brickwork
- Repairing or replacing failed window and door seals
- Repairing cracks in external rendering

Once the source of water ingress is repaired, allow the affected area to dry out fully before redecorating. Depending on the extent of the water damage, this may take several weeks.

### Condensation and Mould Treatment

For condensation-related mould, the priority is improving ventilation and reducing cold surfaces:

- Install or upgrade extractor fans
- Install trickle vents if absent
- Consider PIV systems for persistent cases
- Improve insulation on cold walls and ceilings
- Ensure the heating system is adequate

For the mould itself, affected plaster may need to be removed and replaced if the mould has penetrated the surface. Surface mould can be treated with professional-grade fungicidal wash before redecorating with anti-mould paint. In severe cases, a specialist mould remediation contractor may be needed.

## Your Legal Obligations

Several pieces of legislation create duties on landlords relating to damp and mould.

### Awaab's Law Response Timelines

Under the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents), Awaab's Law is being extended to the private rented sector. The law is named after two-year-old Awaab Ishak, who died in 2020 from a respiratory condition caused by mould in his family's social housing flat. Once in force, the expected timescales require landlords to:

- **Investigate** the reported hazard within **14 calendar days** of being notified by the tenant — failure to investigate within 14 days can result in rent repayment orders of up to 12 months' rent and civil penalties under the [Housing and Planning Act 2016](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2016/22/part/2/chapter/2)
- **Begin repairs** within **7 calendar days** of the investigation for hazards that pose a serious risk to health — missing this deadline triggers the same penalties and exposes you to criminal prosecution under the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/part/1) where the hazard is rated Category 1
- **Complete non-emergency repairs** within a **reasonable timeframe**, with the expectation that straightforward remedial work should not be left open-ended — leaving repairs incomplete for more than 28 days without documented justification creates enforcement risk
- Take action within **24 hours** where a hazard presents an immediate risk to life — failure to act on emergency hazards results in criminal prosecution and unlimited fines

Failure to comply with these timescales can result in civil penalties of up to £30,000 under the [Housing and Planning Act 2016](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2016/22/part/2/chapter/2), rent repayment orders requiring you to repay up to 12 months' rent, and in cases where the hazard constitutes a Category 1 risk, criminal prosecution under the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/part/1). The Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) is the assessment framework used by local authority environmental health officers to determine whether damp and mould constitutes a Category 1 (serious) or Category 2 (less serious) hazard — Category 1 hazards require immediate remedial action and carry the highest penalties.

### Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018

The [Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2018/34/contents) (amending the [Landlord and Tenant Act 1985](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1985/70/contents)) requires that residential properties are fit for human habitation at the start of and throughout a tenancy. Damp and mould growth is one of the specific factors the court considers when assessing fitness under Section 10 of the 1985 Act.

If a property is found to be unfit due to damp and mould, tenants can bring a claim in the county court for:

- An order requiring the landlord to carry out repairs
- **Compensation** for the harm suffered, including damage to belongings, health impacts, and loss of enjoyment of their home

Awards in these cases have been increasing significantly, with some county court and tribunal decisions resulting in compensation running into thousands of pounds.

### The Decent Homes Standard

The [Decent Homes Standard](/blog/decent-homes-standard-private-landlords-england) is being extended to the private rented sector. A property with significant damp and mould is unlikely to meet the standard, which requires that homes are free from Category 1 hazards under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) as defined in the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/part/1). Damp and mould growth is assessed as a specific hazard under the [HHSRS Operating Guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/housing-health-and-safety-rating-system-guidance-for-landlords-and-property-related-professionals), and severe cases are rated as Category 1. The Decent Homes Standard means that any property with Category 1 damp or mould hazards will fail compliance and cannot be lawfully let until remediated.

## Tenant Communication: Responding to Damp Reports

How you respond to a damp report can determine whether the issue is resolved quickly or escalates into a legal dispute. Follow these principles:

### Acknowledge Immediately

When a tenant reports damp or mould, acknowledge receipt of the report within 24 hours. This does not require you to have completed an investigation or decided on a remedy — it simply confirms that you have received their report and are taking it seriously.

### Investigate Within the Legal Timeframe

Under Awaab's Law, you must investigate within 14 days. In practice, this means arranging an inspection, ideally by a qualified surveyor if the cause is not immediately obvious. Document the inspection with photographs, notes on the likely cause, and your proposed course of action.

### Communicate Your Findings

Once you have identified the type of damp and the remedial work required, communicate this to the tenant in writing. Explain:

- What type of damp has been identified
- What is causing it
- What work you will carry out to fix it
- When the work will begin and how long it will take
- What (if anything) the tenant can do to help prevent recurrence

Be clear and factual. If the damp is partly caused by tenant behaviour (for example, blocking vents or refusing to use extractor fans), explain this, but only after you have confirmed that the property has adequate ventilation, heating, and insulation in the first place.

### Begin and Complete Repairs Promptly

For serious hazards, you must begin repairs within 7 days of your investigation. For less serious issues, begin within a reasonable timeframe and keep the tenant informed of progress. Delays create legal risk and tenant dissatisfaction.

### Document Everything

Keep records of:

- The tenant's initial report (date, description, photographs if provided)
- Your acknowledgement
- Inspection reports and photographs
- Contractor quotes and invoices
- Correspondence with the tenant throughout

If the matter escalates to tribunal or court, contemporaneous records are your best defence.

## Practical Example: Responding to a Condensation Complaint

A tenant in a 2-bed flat in Birmingham emails you on 15 October reporting black mould on the bedroom wall behind the wardrobe. Here is how to respond:

**Day 1 (15 Oct):** Acknowledge the email immediately. 'Thank you for reporting the mould. I will arrange an inspection within the next 7 days to identify the cause and the necessary remedial work.'

**Day 5 (19 Oct):** Inspect the property. You find condensation on the bedroom window, mould growth in the corner of an external wall, and the trickle vent on the bedroom window painted shut. The extractor fan in the bathroom is working. The heating is adequate. The loft insulation is minimal.

**Day 6 (20 Oct):** Write to the tenant: 'Following my inspection on 19 October, I have identified that the mould is caused by condensation. This is due to the trickle vent being painted shut and insufficient loft insulation. I will arrange for the vent to be freed and the loft insulation to be upgraded to current standards. I will also treat the mould and redecorate the affected wall. Work will begin on 25 October and should be completed by 1 November. In the meantime, please continue to use the bathroom extractor fan and open the bedroom window for 10-15 minutes each morning to help reduce moisture levels.'

**Day 10 (25 Oct):** Contractor begins work.

**Day 17 (1 Nov):** Work completed. Follow up with the tenant to confirm satisfactory completion and provide written guidance on managing condensation going forward.

This approach meets the legal timeframes, treats the tenant respectfully, addresses the root cause, and documents every step.

## When to Involve Professionals

You should instruct a qualified professional in the following circumstances:

- **Rising damp:** Always use a specialist damp surveyor. DIY diagnosis of rising damp is unreliable, and the wrong treatment is expensive.
- **Persistent condensation despite adequate ventilation and heating:** A building surveyor or hygrothermal specialist can assess whether there are underlying building fabric issues contributing to the problem.
- **Extensive mould growth or suspected hidden mould:** Specialist mould remediation contractors can safely remove and treat extensive mould, particularly where it has penetrated building materials.
- **Health concerns:** If the tenant reports health problems that may be linked to the damp or mould, take this extremely seriously. Arrange an urgent inspection and remedial work, and document your response thoroughly.

## Key Takeaways

- **Damp comes in three types:** rising (structural), penetrating (building maintenance), and condensation (ventilation/heating). Correct diagnosis is essential.
- **Mould is a symptom:** Treat the underlying moisture problem first, then address the mould.
- **Legal timelines are strict:** Investigate within 14 days, begin serious repairs within 7 days, or face penalties of up to £30,000 and rent repayment orders of up to 12 months' rent.
- **Prevention is cheaper than cure:** Adequate ventilation, insulation, and heating prevent most condensation problems.
- **Document everything:** From the initial tenant report through to completion of remedial work.
- **Communicate clearly and promptly:** Acknowledge reports within 24 hours, explain your findings and proposed action, and keep tenants informed throughout.

Damp and mould are serious, common, and heavily regulated. Get ahead of them with routine maintenance and rapid response when they are reported. The cost of prevention is a fraction of the cost of litigation, rent repayment orders, and emergency remediation.
