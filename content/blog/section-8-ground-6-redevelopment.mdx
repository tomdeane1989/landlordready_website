## Executive Summary

Ground 6 of the Housing Act 1988 allows landlords to recover possession to demolish, reconstruct, or substantially alter a rental property in England. It is a mandatory ground, meaning courts must grant it if criteria are met. You must prove the works cannot be done with the tenant in situ, have genuine intent, provide appropriate compensation (under the Renters' Rights Act 2025), and give prescribed notice. Evidence requirements are high: planning permission, architectural drawings, contractor quotes, and proof of funds are typically needed. The notice period is four months under the current Renters' Rights Act framework.

## What Is Ground 6 and When Can You Use It?

Ground 6 is a mandatory possession ground under [Schedule 2 to the Housing Act 1988](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1988/50/schedule/2) that allows private landlords in England to recover their property when demolition, reconstruction, or substantial works make vacant possession essential. Sometimes a rental property reaches a point where routine maintenance is no longer enough. The building may need major structural work, a full refurbishment that cannot be carried out with tenants in situ, or even demolition and rebuilding.

Under Schedule 2 to the Housing Act 1988, Ground 6 is a **mandatory ground** — if you can demonstrate that you meet the requirements, the court must grant possession. This means courts have no discretion; they must award possession if the criteria are satisfied. However, the evidential bar is high, and the Renters' Rights Act 2025 (in force from May 2026) has introduced additional tenant protections, including a statutory right to compensation. Tenants must now be offered compensation before you can enforce possession.

<PullQuote>Ground 6 exists because some works simply cannot be carried out with tenants living in the property. The law recognises this, but it demands genuine evidence of intent.</PullQuote>

## What Works Qualify for Ground 6 Possession?

Ground 6 applies to demolition, reconstruction, or substantial works that cannot be completed with tenants in occupation. According to Schedule 2 to the Housing Act 1988, Ground 6 covers situations where the landlord intends to:

- **Demolish** the property or a substantial part of it
- **Reconstruct** the property or a substantial part of it
- **Carry out substantial works** on the property or a substantial part of it
