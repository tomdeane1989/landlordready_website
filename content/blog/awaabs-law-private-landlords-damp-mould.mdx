## In Short: What Awaab's Law Means for Private Landlords

Awaab's Law now applies to private landlords in England from **1 May 2026**. When a tenant reports damp, mould, or another serious hazard, you must respond within strict timescales: **24 hours** to make safe any hazard presenting an immediate risk to life; **14 days** to acknowledge and investigate non-emergency hazards such as significant damp and mould; **7 days** from the investigation to begin remedial work; and full repairs completed within **4–8 weeks** depending on complexity. 

Failure to comply carries serious consequences. **A first breach triggers a civil penalty of up to £7,000.** Repeated breaches can reach **£40,000.** Tenants can also claim up to **12 months' rent via repayment orders** through the First-tier Tribunal. In the most serious cases—particularly where inaction leads to harm—you face criminal prosecution, with unlimited fines and potential imprisonment. This guide covers the response workflow, what counts as a prescribed hazard, the penalties, and a practical compliance framework.

## Why Awaab's Law Matters: The Story Behind the Legislation

In December 2020, two-year-old Awaab Ishak died from a respiratory condition caused by prolonged exposure to mould in his family's housing association flat in Rochdale. Despite repeated complaints from his parents, the mould was never adequately addressed. The coroner's report attributed his death to persistent damp and mould, as documented in the [Rochdale Safeguarding Partnership review](https://www.rochdaleonline.co.uk/news-features/2/news-headlines/146534/serious-case-review-published-into-death-of-awaab-ishak). The coroner's findings were damning, and the public outcry led directly to legislative action.
