## In Short: What Awaab's Law Means for Private Landlords

Awaab's Law now applies to private landlords in England from **1 May 2026**. When a tenant reports damp, mould, or another serious hazard, you must respond within strict timescales: **24 hours** to make safe any hazard presenting an immediate risk to life; **14 days** to acknowledge and investigate non-emergency hazards such as significant damp and mould; **7 days** from the investigation to begin remedial work; and full repairs completed within **4–8 weeks** depending on complexity. 

Failure to comply carries serious consequences. **A first breach triggers a civil penalty of up to £7,000.** Repeated breaches can reach **£40,000.** Tenants can also claim up to **12 months' rent via repayment orders** through the First-tier Tribunal. In the most serious cases—particularly where inaction leads to harm—you face criminal prosecution, with unlimited fines and potential imprisonment. This guide covers the response workflow, what counts as a prescribed hazard, the penalties, and a practical compliance framework.

## Why Awaab's Law Matters: The Story Behind the Legislation

In December 2020, two-year-old Awaab Ishak died from a respiratory condition caused by prolonged exposure to mould in his family's housing association flat in Rochdale. Despite repeated complaints from his parents, the mould was never adequately addressed. The coroner's report attributed his death to persistent damp and mould, as documented in the [Rochdale Safeguarding Partnership review](https://www.rochdaleonline.co.uk/news-features/2/news-headlines/146534/serious-case-review-published-into-death-of-awaab-ishak). The coroner's findings were damning, and the public outcry led directly to legislative action.

"Awaab's Law" was first introduced through the [Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/36/contents), imposing strict timescales on social landlords for investigating and remedying damp, mould, and other serious hazards. The [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents) extends these duties to the private rented sector in England. For a comprehensive overview of the broader regulatory changes, see our [complete guide to the Renters' Rights Act](/blog/renters-rights-act-2025-what-landlords-need-to-know).

<PullQuote>The principle behind Awaab's Law is simple: when a tenant reports a serious hazard, the landlord must act — and must act quickly.</PullQuote>

## How Awaab's Law Applies to Private Landlords

According to the [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/12/contents) and consultation documents published by the Department for Levelling Up, Housing and Communities in January 2026, the prescribed timescales are expected to come into force from **1 May 2026**. The Act gives the Secretary of State the power to set prescribed timescales within which private landlords in England must investigate and remedy hazards that pose a risk to the health or safety of their tenants. The regulations are expected to mirror those already in force for social housing.

<LegalBlock citation="Section 17, Renters' Rights Act 2025">The Secretary of State may by regulations require a landlord under a qualifying tenancy to comply with specified requirements as to the investigation and resolution of prescribed hazards within prescribed periods.</LegalBlock>

The housing charity Shelter has noted that private landlords should prepare for these timescales now, given the government's stated timeline and the social housing precedent.

<Callout variant="warning">Although the detailed regulations for the private rented sector are still being finalised, the enabling provisions are in the Act. Landlords should prepare now on the basis that the social housing timescales will apply, or something very close to them.</Callout>

## The Expected Timescales

Based on the social housing framework and government consultations as of June 2026, private landlords are expected to comply with the following response timescales once the regulations are in force. The final private rented sector regulations are expected to be published in the first half of 2026, with enforcement from **1 May 2026**. The most current text will appear on [legislation.gov.uk](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/) and the [GOV.UK Renters' Rights Act guidance pages](https://www.gov.uk/government/collections/renters-rights-act-guidance).

| Hazard Type | Response Window | Action Required |
|-------------|-----------------|------------------|
| **Emergency (Risk to Life)** | **24 hours** | Make the situation safe immediately (e.g. carbon monoxide leak, structural collapse) |
| **Non-Emergency (Serious Hazards)** | **14 days** | Acknowledge the tenant's report |
| **Investigation** | **14 days** | Investigate the hazard (from date of report) |
| **Begin Remedial Work** | **7 days** | Start repairs (from completion of investigation) |
| **Complete Repairs** | **4–8 weeks** | Finish all remedial work (straightforward cases at 4 weeks; complex structural work up to 8 weeks, with written updates to tenant) |

### Emergency Hazards (Risk to Life)

Where a hazard presents an immediate risk to life — for example, a carbon monoxide leak (see our guide on [smoke and CO alarm regulations](/blog/smoke-carbon-monoxide-alarm-regulations-landlords)) or structural collapse — the landlord must take action within **24 hours** to make the situation safe.

### Non-Emergency but Serious Hazards

For hazards that are serious but not immediately life-threatening — including significant damp and mould — the expected timescales are:

- **Acknowledge the report** within **14 days**
- **Investigate the hazard** within **14 days** of the report
- **Begin remedial work** within **7 days** of the investigation
- **Complete repairs** within a **reasonable period** — based on the social housing framework, this is expected to mean **4–8 weeks** depending on the complexity of the work. Straightforward mould treatment should be at the shorter end; structural works (e.g. replacing a damp-proof course, re-roofing) will sit at the longer end, with the landlord required to keep the tenant informed in writing.

### What Counts as a "Prescribed Hazard"?

The regulations are expected to cover hazards assessed under the **Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS)** — the statutory framework used by local authority environmental health officers in England and Wales to assess risks to health and safety arising from the condition of residential property. Hazards are scored on the likelihood of harm and the severity of outcome, then placed in Category 1 (most serious — local authority has a duty to act) or Category 2 (serious but discretionary). The full HHSRS list runs to 29 hazards; Awaab's Law focuses in particular on:

- **Damp and mould growth** — the most common and the most politically visible
- **Excess cold** — inadequate heating or insulation (improving your [EPC rating](/blog/epc-rating-requirements-rental-property-2026) can help address this)
- **Structural collapse** — instability in the building fabric
- **Carbon monoxide and fuel combustion products**
- **Falls** — dangerous stairs, balconies, or windows

## Understanding Damp and Mould

Damp and mould are by far the most common hazard that Awaab's Law is designed to address. As a landlord, you need to understand the different types and your responsibilities for each. For detailed prevention strategies and remediation workflows, see our [comprehensive damp and mould guide](/blog/damp-mould-landlord-guide-prevention-legal-duties).

### Rising Damp

Caused by groundwater migrating up through the building fabric due to a defective or absent damp-proof course. This is a structural issue and is always the landlord's responsibility.

### Penetrating Damp

Caused by water entering through the building envelope — typically through damaged roofing, faulty guttering, cracked rendering, or defective window seals. Again, this is a structural maintenance issue and falls squarely on the landlord.

### Condensation

This is where disputes most commonly arise. Condensation occurs when warm, moist air meets a cold surface. It can be caused by:

- **Inadequate ventilation** (landlord's responsibility)
- **Insufficient heating provision** (landlord's responsibility if the heating system is defective or inadequate)
- **Tenant lifestyle factors** (drying clothes indoors without ventilation, blocking air vents)

<Callout variant="tip">Under Awaab's Law, you cannot dismiss a damp or mould complaint simply by blaming the tenant's lifestyle. You must investigate and determine whether there is a structural or maintenance cause. If there is, you must fix it regardless of any contributing tenant behaviour.</Callout>

### When Mould Becomes Dangerous

Mould is not merely a cosmetic issue. Certain moulds produce mycotoxins and allergens that can cause or worsen:

- Asthma and other respiratory conditions
- Allergic reactions (rhinitis, skin rashes)
- Infections in immunocompromised individuals

The HHSRS rates damp and mould as a Category 1 hazard (the most serious category) when the risk to health is significant. A Category 1 hazard triggers a duty on the local authority to take enforcement action.

## What Happens If You Fail to Comply

The penalties for failing to meet the prescribed timescales are designed to be severe enough to ensure compliance.

### Local Authority Enforcement

Environmental health officers already have the power to serve improvement notices and prohibition orders under the [Housing Act 2004](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2004/34/contents). Awaab's Law adds prescribed timescales to these existing powers, making enforcement more straightforward.

### Civil Penalties

Local authorities can impose civil penalties of up to **£7,000** for a first breach and up to **£40,000** for a subsequent breach. These are alternatives to criminal prosecution.

### Rent Repayment Orders

If a landlord fails to comply with an improvement notice, the tenant (or the local authority) can apply to the First-tier Tribunal for a **rent repayment order** — requiring the landlord to repay up to 12 months' rent. This applies to all rental properties in England. The Renters' Rights Act extends the availability of rent repayment orders to cover a wider range of offences.

### Criminal Prosecution

In the most serious cases — particularly where a failure to act leads to harm — landlords can face criminal prosecution under the Housing Act 2004 or, in extreme cases, under health and safety legislation. Unlimited fines and imprisonment are possible.

## A Practical Framework for Compliance

Here is how to build Awaab's Law compliance into your property management.

<StepList>
<Step number={1}>**Create a clear reporting channel.** Make it easy for your tenants to report damp, mould, or other hazards. An email address or online form with automatic acknowledgement is ideal. The clock starts when the tenant reports the issue, so you need to know exactly when that happens.</Step>
<Step number={2}>**Acknowledge promptly.** Respond to the tenant within 14 days (and ideally much sooner) confirming you have received their report and outlining next steps.</Step>
<Step number={3}>**Investigate properly.** Arrange an inspection by a qualified person. For damp and mould, this may be a specialist surveyor rather than a general builder. Identify the root cause — not just the symptoms.</Step>
<Step number={4}>**Document everything.** Take photographs, record moisture readings, note the extent of the mould growth, and record the date and findings of every inspection. This documentation protects you if the case is referred to a Tribunal or enforcement officer.</Step>
<Step number={5}>**Carry out repairs within the prescribed timescale.** Instruct contractors promptly and follow up to ensure the work is completed on time. If the repair is complex and will take longer, communicate this to the tenant in writing and explain the timeline.</Step>
<Step number={6}>**Follow up after completion.** Check that the repair has resolved the issue. Damp and mould can recur if the underlying cause has not been fully addressed. A follow-up inspection a few weeks later is good practice.</Step>
</StepList>

## Proactive Property Maintenance

The best way to comply with Awaab's Law is to prevent hazards from arising in the first place.

### Ventilation

Ensure every property has adequate ventilation. Extractor fans in bathrooms and kitchens should be in working order. Trickle vents on windows should not be sealed or blocked. Consider installing mechanical ventilation in properties that are particularly prone to condensation.

### Heating

The heating system must be adequate to heat the property to a reasonable temperature. A property that cannot be heated to at least 18 degrees Celsius in living areas is likely to develop condensation problems. Ensure your [gas safety certificate](/blog/gas-safety-certificate-landlord-guide-2026) is current and that boilers are serviced regularly.

### Building Fabric

Inspect roofs, guttering, rendering, and window seals regularly. Penetrating damp is almost always a maintenance failure that can be prevented with routine upkeep.

### Tenant Communication

Provide tenants with clear guidance on ventilation and heating — not as a way to shift blame, but as a genuine effort to help them live comfortably and reduce condensation risk. This complements your structural obligations; it does not replace them.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What counts as a prescribed hazard under Awaab's Law?**

Prescribed hazards are assessed under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS). They include damp and mould growth, excess cold, structural collapse, carbon monoxide and fuel combustion products, and falls. Category 1 hazards (the most serious) trigger a duty on the local authority to act.

**What if I miss the 14-day investigation deadline?**

Missing the deadline exposes you to civil penalties of up to £7,000 for a first breach and up to £40,000 for subsequent breaches. The tenant or local authority can also apply for a rent repayment order requiring you to repay up to 12 months' rent. In serious cases, you may face criminal prosecution.

**Do I need professional remediation for all damp and mould cases?**

You must investigate properly and identify the root cause. For significant damp and mould, this typically requires a specialist surveyor rather than a general builder. Superficial treatment (e.g. painting over mould) without addressing the underlying cause will not satisfy your legal obligations and the problem will recur.

**Can I blame tenant lifestyle for condensation and mould?**

No. Under Awaab's Law, you cannot dismiss a damp or mould complaint by blaming the tenant's lifestyle. You must investigate and determine whether there is a structural or maintenance cause. If there is, you must fix it regardless of any contributing tenant behaviour.

**What happens if the repair will take longer than 8 weeks?**

If the repair is genuinely complex and will exceed the expected 4–8 week window, you must communicate this to the tenant in writing, explain the timeline, and provide regular updates. However, you should instruct contractors promptly and expedite the work wherever possible. Delays caused by poor project management will not be accepted as a defence.

For landlords who already maintain their properties well and respond promptly to maintenance requests, Awaab's Law will not require a radical change.
