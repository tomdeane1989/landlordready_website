# Awaab's Law and Private Landlords: What Damp and Mould Duties Are Coming

Awaab's Law started in social housing, but it is on its way into the private rented sector. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 gives the government the power to apply the same hazard-response timescales to private landlords that already bind councils and housing associations — covering damp, mould, and other serious risks to tenant health. If you let property in England, this is the regime you'll be working under within the next year or two, and the practical preparation starts now.

## TL;DR

Awaab's Law sets legally enforceable deadlines for landlords to investigate and fix serious hazards in rented homes — most notably damp and mould. It has applied to **social landlords since 27 October 2025** under the Hazards in Social Housing (Prescribed Requirements) (England) Regulations 2025. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 extends the same framework to **private landlords in England**, with timescales to be finalised after consultation (likely from 2027). In the social sector the headline rules are: investigate emergency hazards within 24 hours, investigate significant damp and mould within 10 working days, make the home safe within 5 working days after that, and offer alternative accommodation if you can't. Private landlords should expect a very similar shape.

<Callout variant="info">Today's date: 10 June 2026. Awaab's Law for the private rented sector is not yet in force. The detailed PRS timescales will be set out in regulations following government consultation — confirm the latest position before relying on specific deadlines.</Callout>

## What is Awaab's Law?

Awaab's Law is named after Awaab Ishak, a two-year-old who died in December 2020 after prolonged exposure to black mould in his family's Rochdale social housing flat. The legal framework was created by section 42 of the [Social Housing (Regulation) Act 2023](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2023/36/contents), which inserted a new **section 10A into the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985**. Section 10A implies a term into social tenancy agreements requiring landlords to comply with hazard-response timescales set in secondary legislation.

Those timescales arrived in the [Hazards in Social Housing (Prescribed Requirements) (England) Regulations 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2025/1064/contents/made), in force from 27 October 2025. The regulations bite on social landlords first, but the architecture — an implied tenancy term plus a prescribed timetable — is exactly what the government has signalled it will replicate for the private sector.

## How does Awaab's Law get extended to private landlords?

The [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/32/contents) gives ministers the power to apply Awaab's Law to assured tenancies in the private rented sector. According to the government's [Guide to the Renters' Rights Act](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/guide-to-the-renters-rights-act/guide-to-the-renters-rights-act), the Act "will imply terms into private rented sector tenancy agreements" so that private landlords must meet Awaab's Law requirements once regulations are made — initially focused on hazards such as damp and mould.

The government's [implementation roadmap published in November 2025](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/renters-rights-act-2025-implementation-roadmap/implementing-the-renters-rights-act-2025-our-roadmap-for-reforming-the-private-rented-sector) places the PRS extension of Awaab's Law in **Phase Three**, alongside the modernised Decent Homes Standard. The exact commencement date is, as of 10 June 2026, listed as "TBC – subject to consultation." Shelter and other commentators have suggested 2027 at the earliest. Treat any specific PRS deadline you read online as provisional until the regulations are actually laid.

## What hazards does Awaab's Law cover?

The phased approach in social housing tells you where the PRS rules are likely to land. According to the government's [Awaab's Law guidance for social landlords](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords-timeframes-for-repairs-in-the-social-rented-sector), the phases are:

| Phase | From | Hazards in scope |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 27 October 2025 | All emergency hazards + significant damp and mould |
| Phase 2 | October 2026 | Adds excess cold, excess heat, falls, structural collapse, fire, electrical, explosions, hygiene |
| Phase 3 | October 2027 | All remaining Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS) hazards, except overcrowding |

The categories come from the **Housing Health and Safety Rating System (HHSRS)** — the same risk framework councils already use to inspect privately rented homes. A "significant hazard" is defined in the regulations as one posing "a risk of harm to the occupier's health or safety that a reasonable lessor with the relevant knowledge would take steps to make safe as a matter of urgency." An "emergency hazard" is one posing an imminent and significant risk — think a gas leak, a major roof leak over a bedroom, or a dangerous electrical fault.

## What are the actual timescales?

These are the timescales that apply to social landlords today and that PRS landlords should treat as a strong indication of what is coming. From the [GOV.UK guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords/awaabs-law-guidance-for-social-landlords-timeframes-for-repairs-in-the-social-rented-sector):

<StepList>
<Step number={1}>**Emergency hazards — 24 hours.** Investigate and carry out works to make the property safe as soon as reasonably practicable and within 24 hours of becoming aware of the hazard.</Step>
<Step number={2}>**Significant hazards — 10 working days.** Investigate any potential significant hazard within 10 working days of becoming aware of it.</Step>
<Step number={3}>**Written summary — 3 working days.** Provide the tenant with a written summary of the investigation findings within 3 working days of the investigation concluding.</Step>
<Step number={4}>**Make safe — 5 working days.** Carry out the safety works within 5 working days of the investigation finishing if it confirms a significant hazard.</Step>
<Step number={5}>**Begin further works — within 12 weeks.** Begin any supplementary preventative work (to stop the hazard recurring) within a reasonable timeframe — the social-sector regulations require works to physically begin within 12 weeks at the latest.</Step>
<Step number={6}>**Alternative accommodation.** If the home cannot be made safe within the relevant timescale, offer suitable alternative accommodation at the landlord's expense until it can.</Step>
</StepList>

## What happens if a landlord misses the deadlines?

In social housing, the duty operates as an **implied term of the tenancy agreement** — meaning the tenant can sue the landlord for breach of contract through the courts, with remedies including enforcement orders, compensation, legal costs and potentially loss of rent for the period the home was uninhabitable. The same mechanism is being replicated for private landlords through the Renters' Rights Act 2025.

There is a statutory "reasonable steps" defence — under section 10A(5) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, a landlord has a defence if they have taken all reasonable steps to comply. In practice that defence depends on contemporaneous records: keep a paper trail of tenant communications, contractor quotes, access arrangements and works carried out.

In addition, the Renters' Rights Act 2025 introduces a **legal duty for private landlords to meet the Decent Homes Standard**. The [government's guide to the Act](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/guide-to-the-renters-rights-act/guide-to-the-renters-rights-act) confirms that councils will be able to issue civil penalties of up to **£7,000** to landlords who fail to take reasonably practicable steps to keep properties free of serious hazards. Tenants will also be able to escalate complaints to the new [PRS Landlord Ombudsman](/blog/prs-ombudsman-landlord-membership-requirements).

<Callout variant="warning">Don't wait for the PRS regulations to bite. Damp and mould complaints already trigger enforcement under the Housing Health and Safety Rating System, the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018 and section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985. A slow response to mould is a legal risk *now*, not just in 2027.</Callout>

## What should a private landlord do in 2026?

For a self-managing landlord with one or two properties, the smart preparation is operational, not legal. The regulations will tell you *what* the deadlines are; your job is to make sure you can hit them.

- **Have a written reporting route for tenants.** Email or a portal — something dated and saveable, not a WhatsApp message you can't find six months later.
- **Trial-run your contractor list.** Can you actually get a damp specialist out within 10 working days? Have a primary and a backup. Read our guide on [repair and maintenance responsibilities](/blog/landlord-responsibilities-repairs-maintenance) for the existing duties.
- **Don't blame "lifestyle".** Government guidance is explicit that landlords should not assume damp and mould is the tenant's fault. Investigate the cause: ventilation, insulation, cold bridging, leaks. See our [damp and mould guide](/blog/damp-mould-landlord-guide-prevention-legal-duties) for the diagnostic basics.
- **Document everything.** Photos before, during and after. Dated written summaries to tenants. Invoices from contractors. This is what the "reasonable steps" defence is built on.
- **Know your wider compliance picture.** Awaab's Law sits alongside the [Decent Homes Standard](/blog/decent-homes-standard-private-landlords-england), [EPC requirements](/blog/epc-rating-requirements-rental-property-2026) and the [Renters' Rights Act compliance checklist](/blog/renters-rights-act-compliance-checklist-landlords).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When does Awaab's Law apply to private landlords?

As of 10 June 2026, Awaab's Law applies to social landlords only. The Renters' Rights Act 2025 gives ministers the power to extend it to the private rented sector, but the regulations setting the PRS timescales have not yet been laid. The government has placed PRS implementation in Phase Three of its roadmap and will consult on the detail — most external commentators expect it to take effect from 2027.

### What is the difference between an emergency hazard and a significant hazard?

An emergency hazard poses an imminent and significant risk to health or safety — a major leak, dangerous electrics, a broken external door. A significant hazard poses a serious but not immediately life-threatening risk that a reasonable landlord would still treat as urgent. In social housing, emergency hazards must be investigated and made safe within 24 hours; significant damp and mould hazards within 10 working days, with safety works completed within 5 working days of investigation.

### Will private landlords have to pay for alternative accommodation?

If the PRS rules follow the social housing model — and the government has indicated they will — then yes. Where a serious hazard cannot be remedied within the required timescale, the landlord must offer suitable alternative accommodation at their own expense until the home is safe to return to. This is one of the biggest practical risks to plan for.

### Does Awaab's Law replace section 11 repair duties?

No. Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, the Homes (Fitness for Human Habitation) Act 2018 and the Housing Health and Safety Rating System all continue to apply. Awaab's Law adds **fixed statutory deadlines** on top of existing duties — it does not weaken any of them.

### What penalties can a private landlord face?

Once Awaab's Law is in force for the PRS, tenants will be able to take court action for breach of the implied tenancy term, with potential compensation, enforcement orders and loss of rent. Separately, under the Renters' Rights Act 2025, councils will be able to issue civil penalties of up to £7,000 against landlords who fail to take reasonably practicable steps to keep properties free of serious hazards — and repeat or serious breaches across the wider compliance regime can attract penalties of up to £40,000.
