## What Is the Renters' Rights Act 2025?

The Renters' Rights Act 2025 is the most significant reform to private renting in England in a generation. It abolishes Section 21 no-fault evictions, converts all tenancies to periodic (rolling monthly) agreements, and requires landlords to provide a statutory reason to regain possession. Phase 1 took effect on 1 May 2026, ending fixed-term assured shorthold tenancies and introducing new possession grounds, a mandatory ombudsman scheme, and a national Property Portal. The Act does not prevent you from ending a tenancy — it changes the legal process you must follow to do so.

<PullQuote>The Renters' Rights Act does not prevent you from regaining possession of your property — it changes the process you must follow to do so.</PullQuote>

The [Renters' Rights Act 2025](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2025/26/contents) received Royal Assent in late 2025, replacing the previous framework under the [Housing Act 1988](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1988/50/contents) with a system designed to give tenants greater security of tenure whilst retaining workable routes for landlords to recover their properties when they have legitimate reasons.

## When Does the Renters' Rights Act Take Effect?

Phase 1 of the Renters' Rights Act took effect on **1 May 2026**. This first phase includes the abolition of Section 21, the end of fixed-term assured shorthold tenancies, and the introduction of periodic tenancies. Further provisions — including the Decent Homes Standard, rent repayment order reforms, and [Awaab's Law requirements for private landlords](/blog/awaabs-law-private-landlords-damp-mould) — will follow in later phases.

## What Are the Key Changes for Landlords?

The Act introduces five major changes that affect how you manage your rental properties in England:

### 1. Section 21 No-Fault Evictions Are Abolished

[Section 21 of the Housing Act 1988](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1988/50/section/21) has been repealed. You can no longer serve a Section 21 notice to end an assured shorthold tenancy without giving a reason. Every possession claim must now be based on one of the statutory grounds in Schedule 2 of the Housing Act 1988 (as amended).

Learn how to [end a tenancy after Section 21 is abolished](/blog/how-to-end-tenancy-after-section-21-abolished) using the new possession grounds.

<Callout variant="warning">If you served a Section 21 notice before 1 May 2026 but have not yet obtained a court order, that notice may now be invalid. Consult a solicitor if you have proceedings in progress.</Callout>

### 2. All New Tenancies Must Be Periodic

Fixed-term assured shorthold tenancies no longer exist for new agreements created after 1 May 2026. All tenancies are now [periodic](/blog/periodic-tenancy-explained-landlords) — rolling on a monthly (or other periodic) basis from the start.
